## Case Definition
## Confirmed Case
Foodborne Botulism
Clinical illness (A) with laboratory confirmation of botulism intoxication:
- Detection of botulinum toxin in serum, stool, gastric aspirate or food,(B)
OR
- Isolation of Clostridium (C.) botulinum from stool or gastric aspirate;
OR
Clinical illness(A) and indication that the client ate the same suspect food(B) as an individual with laboratory confirmed botulism
intoxication.
Wound Botulism
Presence of a recent wound infection in the two weeks before symptom onset with laboratory confirmation of intoxication and
no evidence of consumption of food contaminated with C. botulinum:
- Detection of botulinum toxin in serum,
OR
- Isolation of C. botulinum from a wound.
Infant Botulism
Laboratory confirmation of botulism intoxication and symptoms compatible with botulism in a person less than one year of
age(C):
- Detection of botulinum toxin in stool or serum,
OR
- Isolation of C. botulinum from the patient’s stool, or at autopsy.
Colonization Botulism (Intestinal Toxemia)
Laboratory confirmation of botulism intoxication with clinical illness(A) in a patient aged ≥ one year, with severely compromised
gastrointestinal tract functioning (i.e., abnormal bowel) due to various diseases such as colitis, intestinal bypass procedures or
in association with other conditions that may create local or widespread disruption in the normal intestinal flora.
- Detection of botulinum toxin in stool or serum,
OR
- Isolation of C. botulinum from the patient’s stool, or during an autopsy.
(A) Clinical illness is characterized by diplopia, blurred vision, and bulbar weakness. Symmetric paralysis may progress rapidly. See Table 1:
## Clinical Presentation for more information.
(B) Foodborne – identification of organisms in a suspected food is helpful but not diagnostic because botulinum spores are ubiquitous.
Therefore, the presence of toxin in a suspected contaminated food source is more significant.
(C) Clinical illness in infants is characterized by constipation, loss of appetite, weakness, altered cry and loss of head control.

## Probable Case
Foodborne
Clinical illness(A) and consumption of a suspect food item(D) within the incubation period (12–48 hours).
## Suspect Case
Clinical illness(A) in a person without laboratory confirmed botulism intoxication or an epidemiological link (e.g., exposed to the
same source such as food)

(D) Foodborne – identification of organisms in a suspected food is helpful but not diagnostic because botulinum spores are ubiquitous.

Clinical Assessment and Epidemiology
## Etiology
Botulism is caused most commonly by Clostridium botulinum, and rarely by C. baratii, C. butyricum and C. argentinese. These
are gram-positive, spore forming bacteria that produce a lethal neurotoxin, botulinum toxin. The spores formed from the
bacteria are commonly found in the environment but only grow and produce toxins in the following conditions: anaerobic,
temperatures between 3 and 37 degrees Celsius, low acidity, moisture, sugar, and salt. There are seven serotypes (types A-
G); serotypes A, B, E and F can cause illness in humans.(1–3)
## Clinical Presentation
The botulinum neurotoxin (BoNT) blocks nerve functions causing paralysis. Onset is typically with neurological symptoms
involving the cranial nerves causing symptoms such as:
- difficulty in speaking (i.e., stuttering/stammering),
- dry mouth, double vision,
- drooping eyelids (ptosis),
- facial weakness,
- difficulty swallowing, and
- absence of fever.
These symptoms progress as a descending symmetric paralysis starting with weakness in the neck and arms progressing to
other extremities and respiratory muscles. Mechanical ventilation may be required to manage severe respiratory problems.
Symptoms may be sudden or progress gradually over several days.(1,4–7) The overall case fatality rate of botulism can be high
if untreated with antitoxin and supportive care. Administration of antitoxin neutralizes the toxin and stops toxin uptake and
should be given within 24–48 hours of onset of symptoms for maximum benefit. However, antitoxin should be administered
regardless of time since symptom onset to prevent further toxin uptake.(1,8) For clinical symptoms of various types of botulism,
refer to Table 1: Clinical Presentation.

## Diagnosis
Diagnosis is based on high clinical suspicion and a thorough neurological assessment. Myasthenia gravis, stroke and Guillain-
Barré syndrome are part of the differential diagnosis and should be ruled out in order to provide antitoxin treatment as soon as
possible. Laboratory confirmation of botulism intoxication can be obtained with serum and stool assays for BoNT, stool
microscopy for spores, stool cultures, and wound cultures in the case of wound botulism.(2,11,13) Consultation with the
Microbiologist on call at the Public Health Laboratories (ProvLab) is required prior to specimen collection. Testing of suspect
cases should be coordinated through the MOH (or designate). Specimens collected (food and clinical) are sent by ProvLab to
the Botulism Reference Laboratory in Ottawa, Ontario. For more information refer to the ProvLab Guide to Services.
## Treatment
Treatment of suspected botulism cases should be initiated as soon as possible and consists of immediate hospital admission,
antitoxin administration, respiratory support, and close monitoring, as required. Wound botulism requires debridement of the
infected area and antibiotics.(11)
Botulism antitoxin (BAT) is used for the treatment of adults, pediatric and infant cases with suspected, probable and
confirmed botulism.
- Consultation with an infectious disease specialist should be done prior to the administration of BAT Botulism Antitoxin
(BAT) use must be approved and accessed via the Office of the Chief Medical Officer of Health (OCMOH).
- See Appendix 1: Process for the Approval and Release of Botulism Antitoxin
- Refer to the product monograph and the Alberta Immunization Policy (AIP) for detailed instructions regarding access,
dosage and administration for the product supplied.
- NOTE: BabyBIG can be used to treat infant botulism in children under the age of one. However, since BAT has been
authorized for treatment of botulism in all ages and is readily available in the province, this will be the only product in use
in Alberta.
Reservoir
Botulism spores are ubiquitous in soil worldwide. They are frequently recovered from agricultural products, including honey.
Spores are also found in marine sediments and in the intestinal tract of animals and fish.(11,13)

## Period of Communicability
Botulism is not transmitted person-to-person.(1,2)
Host Susceptibility
Susceptibility is universal. Infection with botulism does not confer immunity from future exposures.(1)
Infant botulism affects children younger than one year of age.
- Anyone who eats food contaminated with botulism toxin is at risk of foodborne botulism.
- The risk for wound botulism is higher in people who inject drugs, especially the use of black tar-heroin.
- The risk for iatrogenic botulism is high in those receiving high doses of botulinum toxin for therapeutic treatment.(2,13,14)
- Although rare, adults with a history of bowel surgery or use of antibiotics may be at increased risk for colonization
(intestinal toxemia) botulism.

Incidence
Botulism cases are rare; however, outbreaks of foodborne botulism due to improper food preparation and preservation have
been reported globally.(2)
In Canada, cases of botulism have been nationally notifiable since 1933. The following sources of foodborne botulism have
been identified in Canada: fermented fish/marine mammal meat, improperly stored low acid fruit juices and improperly home-
canned low acid foods. Raw (unpasteurized) honey is a risk for infant botulism. An average of six cases were reported
annually in Canada from 2010-2020.(15) For current case counts in Canada refer to the Notifiable Diseases Online website.
In Alberta, botulism cases have been reported since 1962 and for the past five years, an average of one case has been
reported every year. For more information on reported cases in Alberta, refer to the Interactive Health Data Application (IHDA).

Management of a Case
- Requires immediate emergency medical treatment. See Treatment section
## Treatment must not wait for laboratory confirmation.
- Hospitalized cases should be managed in accordance with hospital infection prevention and control guidelines.
- Provide general education regarding disease transmission and prevention.

Management of Contacts
- Botulism is not transmitted person-to-person; therefore, contact follow-up is not required.
- However, individuals exposed to the same source (e.g., consumed same food source; same aerosolization exposure)
should be assessed for clinical illness and/or advised to seek immediate medical assessment if symptoms develop.
Preventative Measures
- Educate the public on safe food handling as outlined on the food safety tips website to prevent foodborne botulism.
- Infants younger than one year of age should not be fed honey to prevent the potential consumption of C. botulinum spores
- Educate the public on proper wound care to prevent wound botulism. See Care for a Skin Wound and Wounds websites
for more information.
