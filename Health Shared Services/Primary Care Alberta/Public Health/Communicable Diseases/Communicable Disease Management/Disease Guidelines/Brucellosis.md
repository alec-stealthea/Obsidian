## Case Definition – • Defined epidemiological linkage (footnote).
## probable case

Brucellosis
## Case Definition
## Confirmed Case
Clinical illness(A) with laboratory confirmation of infection:
- Isolation of Brucella species from an appropriate clinical specimen (e.g., blood, bone marrow)(B);
OR
- Detection of Brucella nucleic acid (e.g., PCR) from an appropriate clinical specimen (e.g., blood, CSF, biopsy tissue)(C);
OR
- Seroconversion or significant (i.e., fourfold or greater) rise in Brucella agglutination titre between acute and convalescent
phase serum specimens obtained two or more weeks apart.
## Probable Case
Clinical illness(A) and epidemiological-linkage(D)(1);
OR
Clinical illness(A) and supportive serology (Brucella agglutination titre of 1:160 or higher in one or more serum specimens
obtained after onset of symptoms).
(A) Clinical illness: see Clinical Presentation.
(B) Refer to the Brucellosis - Public Health (ProvLab) for current specimen collection and submission information.
(C) Refer to the National Microbiology Laboratory (NML) Guide to Services for current specimen collection and submission information.
(D) Epidemiologically linked includes:
- Direct contact with body fluids or tissue from a confirmed human case of brucellosis, OR
- Veterinary occupational exposure to Brucella vaccine (i.e., needle stick, mucous membrane exposure), OR
- Laboratory exposure to brucellosis-causing Brucella species (BBS), OR
- Direct contact to an animal diagnosed with a Brucella infection (or their fluids), as determined by a veterinarian or the Provincial Health
Veterinarian, including potential aerosol exposure, OR
- Shared one of the following exposures with a confirmed human case of brucellosis:
- Consumption of dairy products from a common source that were unpasteurized or of unknown pasteurization, OR
- Consumption or handling of undercooked meat or carcass of an animal from a herd or of a species with a known or suspected
history of Brucella, OR
- Slaughtering, dressing, butchering, or having other direct contact with animals or animal tissues possibly infected with Brucella.

## Reporting Requirements
Physicians, Health Practitioners and Others
Physicians, health practitioners and others shall notify the Zone Medical Officer of Health (MOH) (or designate), of all
confirmed and probable cases in the prescribed form by mail, fax or electronic submission (e.g., email) within 48 hours (two
business days).
Laboratories
All laboratories shall report all positive laboratory results by mail, fax or electronic submission (e.g., HL7 message) within 48
hours (two business days) to the:
- Zone MOH (or designate), and
- Chief Medical Officer of Health (CMOH) (or designate)
Primary Care Alberta and Indigenous Services Canada
- The Zone MOH (or designate) where the case currently resides shall forward the initial Notifiable Disease Report (NDR)
of all confirmed and probable cases to the CMOH (or designate) within two weeks of notification and the final NDR
(amendment) within four weeks of notification.
- For out-of-province and/or out-of-country cases and/or contacts, the Zone MOH (or designate) should forward the
following information to the CMOH (or designate) by phone, fax or electronic submission within 48 hours (two business
days):
name,
date of birth,
out-of-province health care number,
out-of-province address and phone number,
positive laboratory report (if applicable), and
other relevant clinical/epidemiological information.
Additional Reporting Requirements
Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA)
Under the Health of Animals Act – Reportable Diseases Regulations, all animal cases of Brucellosis must be reported to CFIA.
If there are known or suspected animal sources of a human brucellosis case, the Zone MOH (or designate) should report them
to the District Veterinarian of the CFIA and CMOH (or designate). CFIA contact information is available here.

Clinical Assessment and Epidemiology
## Etiology
Brucellosis is caused by the gram-negative coccobacillus Brucella.(2) The species known to infect humans are Brucella abortus
(from cattle), Brucella melitensis (from sheep and goats), Brucella suis (from swine) and Brucella. canis (from dogs).(2,3)
## Clinical Presentation
The onset of brucellosis may be acute or insidious with symptoms such as intermittent fever, headache, weakness, profuse
sweating, chills, arthralgia, depression, weight loss, and generalized aching.(2,4) Local infections of organs (e.g., liver, spleen,
etc.) may be present.(4) Serious complications include neurobrucellosis, endocarditis and osteomyelitis.(5) Brucellosis can
occasionally last up to a year or more if not adequately treated.(4) Untreated cases have a case fatality rate of less than 2%.(4)
## Diagnosis
The gold standard of brucellosis diagnosis is confirmation of Brucella species from either blood, bone marrow, other tissue
specimens by culture.(2,4) Consultation with the Microbiologist on call is required prior to specimen collection. Notify the lab
prior to specimen collection and indicate the suspected organism on the requisition. For more information, refer to the
Brucellosis - Public Health (ProvLab).
## Treatment
Brucellosis is treatable by antimicrobial therapy. Consultation with an infectious disease specialist is recommended.
Reservoir
The most common animal reservoirs are cattle, swine, sheep, and goats.(4) Other reservoirs may include camels, bison, elk,
caribou, moose, wild hogs, dogs and coyotes.(4)
## Transmission
Transmission occurs through ingestion (eating or drinking unpasteurized dairy products), direct contact with bodily fluids of
infected animals via skin abrasions and mucous membranes, and inhalation.(4) Airborne transmission has been reported in in
## laboratory and slaughterhouse workers.(4)
Person-to-person transmission is rare.(4) People who are breastfeeding may transmit infection to their infants.(4) Although rare,
## transmission may also occur through blood transfusions, tissue transplants, or sexually.(4)
Brucellosis has the potential to be used as a bioterrorist agent due to its potential to be aerosolized.(4)
## Incubation Period
The incubation period is highly variable; generally 1–2 months, with a range of 5 days to 5 months.(4)
## Period of Communicability
Unknown. Person-to-person spread is rare.(4)

Host Susceptibility
Susceptibility is universal.
Incidence
Brucellosis occurs worldwide but particularly in the Canadian Arctic, India, Central Asia, Africa, the Mediterranean, the Middle
East, Central/South America and Mexico.(4)
Brucellosis has been a nationally notifiable disease in Canada since 1928.(1) For the current incidence in Canada refer to the
Notifiable Diseases Online website.
To date, all cases reported in Alberta have been related to travel abroad or residence outside of Alberta (e.g., Canadian Arctic)
or Canada. For the current incidence in Alberta, refer to the Interactive Health Data Application (IHDA).
Brucellosis is not endemic in Canadian domestic livestock, and Canada was declared free of bovine brucellosis in 1985 but
there are two wildlife reservoirs where the disease persists: free-ranging bison herds in and around Wood Buffalo National
Park (located at the border between Alberta and the Northwest Territories), and free-roaming caribou and reindeer in the arctic
and sub-arctic regions of Canada.(6) These reservoirs are managed in order to prevent the spread of brucellosis to domestic
livestock, maintaining Canada's brucellosis-free status for livestock.(6) For more information on brucellosis in livestock and
wildlife, refer to the CFIA website.

## Public Health Management
Key Investigation
- Confirm that the individual meets the case definition.
- Obtain a history of illness, including date of onset and signs and symptoms.
- Determine possible source of infection, considering the incubation period, reservoir and mode of transmission, and obtain
a history of:
travel to or immigration from areas where the disease is endemic in animals (mainly cattle, sheep, and goats),
consumption of unpasteurized dairy products or raw meat,
occupational risk (e.g., laboratory worker, veterinarian, meat packing plant/slaughterhouse worker, wildlife
biologist/officer), and
outdoor/lifestyle risk (e.g., hunter).(4,7)
Management of a Case
- Hospitalized cases should be managed in accordance with hospital infection prevention and control policies.
- Supportive care should be provided based on clinical presentation and dependent on laboratory results.
- Provide general education regarding disease transmission and prevention.
Management of Contacts
- Since brucellosis is rarely transmitted person-to-person, contact follow-up is not generally required.
- However, individuals exposed to the same source (e.g., common exposure to infected animals) should be assessed for
clinical illness. Advise them to seek medical evaluation if symptoms develop.
Preventive Measures
- Currently, no human vaccine is available for brucellosis.(3)
- Educate people on safe food practices, including:
Avoid consuming unpasteurized dairy products (e.g., milk, cheese, ice cream), especially when traveling abroad,
Refrain from eating uncooked animal viscera, and
Cook all domestic and wild meats thoroughly.(4)
- Educate people at risk of exposure (e.g., hunters, animal handlers, farmers, or workers in slaughterhouses, meat
processing plants and butcher shops) to:
Wear protective gear when handling potentially infected animals, for example:

rubber gloves

protective clothing

face shield and mask.
Handle animal carcasses in well-ventilated areas.
Dispose of discarded animal remains properly by burying.
Exercise caution when handling and disposing of the animal placenta, discharges, and aborted fetuses.
Disinfect contaminated areas thoroughly.(4)
- Laboratory workers should adhere to their occupational health and safety protocols.
