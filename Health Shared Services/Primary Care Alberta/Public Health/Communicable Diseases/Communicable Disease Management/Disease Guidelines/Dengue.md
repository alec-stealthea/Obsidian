## Case Definition
## Confirmed Case
## Laboratory confirmation of infection with clinical illness(A):
- Detection of Dengue virus (DENV) nucleic acid (e.g., by RT-PCR) or isolation from an appropriate clinical specimen
(e.g., blood, CSF, urine);
OR
- Positive DENV IgM antibody with DENV IgG seroconversion and the following were not completed: ZIKV IgM/IgG and
PRNT testing; (B)
OR
- Positive DENV IgM antibody with or without DENV IgG seroconversion AND a positive (≥1:20) DENV Plaque
Reduction Neutralization test (PRNT) where the ZIKV PRNT is negative; (B)
## Probable Case
## Laboratory evidence of infection with clinical illness(A):
- Positive DENV IgM antibody with or without positive DENV IgG antibody and the following were not completed: ZIKV
IgM/IgG and PRNT testing.(B)
(Refer to Table 1 for more information).
(A) Clinical illness: refer to Clinical Presentation.
(B) Due to declining ZIKV activity worldwide, PRNT testing for flaviviruses such as dengue and zika will only be requested when ZIKV is
suspected (usually in congenital cases). Refer to Table 1 for assistance with interpreting lab results and/or consult the Virologist-on-call.

## Reporting Requirements
Physicians, Health Practitioners and Others
- Physicians, health practitioners or others shall notify the Medical Officer of Health (MOH) (or designate) of the zone, of all
confirmed and probable cases by mail, fax or electronic transfer within 48 hours (two business days).
Laboratories
- Laboratories shall report all positive laboratory results by mail, fax or electronic transfer within 48 hours (two business
days) to the MOH (or designate) of the zone and the Chief Medical Officer of Health (CMOH) (or designate).
Alberta Health Services and First Nations Inuit Health Branch Public Health
- The MOH/designate of the zone where the case currently resides shall report all confirmed and probable cases to the
CMOH/designate using the Notifiable Disease Report (NDR) form or another mutually agreed upon reporting system
within two weeks of notification for the initial NDR and the final NDR (amendment) within four weeks, as per usual.
- For out-of-province and out-of-country cases, the following information should be forwarded to the CMOH/or designate as
per usual communicable disease reporting processes and include:
- name,
- date of birth,
- out-of-province health care number,
- out-of-province address and phone number,
- any positive laboratory report/s, and
- other relevant clinical / epidemiological information.

## Epidemiology
## Etiology
DENV is a member of the Flaviviridae family.(2,3) There are four closely related but antigenically distinct virus serotypes: DEN-
1, DEN-2, DEN-3, and DEN-4.
## Clinical Presentation
Dengue infection is a systemic and dynamic disease. It has a wide clinical spectrum which includes both severe and non-
severe clinical manifestations. The majority of individuals have no symptoms, especially children and those who have not had
DENV previously.(4) Illness begins abruptly and has three phases -- febrile, critical and recovery.
Dengue: Usually lasts 2-7 days;(5)
Definition: fever and any two of the following:
- nausea/vomiting,
- rash,
- myalgia, arthralgia,
- leukopenia, and
- minor hemorrhagic manifestations (e.g., petechiae, ecchymosis, purpura, epistaxis, bleeding gums, hematuria, or
a positive tourniquet test).
Dengue with Warning Signs**: progression to severe dengue begins to occur in the late febrile phase around the time of
defervescence and includes any of the following:(5)
Definition: Dengue with any of the following
- abdominal pain or tenderness,
- persistent vomiting,
- Clinical fluid accumulation (ascites, pleural effusion),
- Mucosal bleeding,
- Lethargy, restlessness,
- Liver enlargement >2 cm, and
- Laboratory: increase in hematocrit concurrent with rapid decrease in platelet count.
**requires strict observation and medical intervention
Severe Dengue: In the critical phase individuals with severe dengue develop severe plasma leakage as a result of a
marked increase in vascular permeability. Intense medical intervention is required to aid their physiologic compensatory
mechanisms in maintaining adequate circulation. However, once hypotension develops, systolic blood pressure rapidly
declines, and irreversible shock and death may ensue despite resuscitation.
Definition: Dengue with at least one of the following criteria (includes Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever & Dengue Shock
Syndrome [DSS]):(5)
- Severe Plasma Leakage leading to:
- DSS
- Fluid accumulation with respiratory distress
- Severe Bleeding as evaluated by clinician
- Severe organ involvement
- Liver: AST or ALT ≥ 1000
- CNS: impaired consciousness
- Failure of heart and other organs
Mortality rates from severe dengue can be as high as 20% if untreated.(6) Pregnant women with DENV are at increased
risk of miscarriage, premature birth and infants with low birth weight.(7)

## Diagnosis
PCR testing by Alberta Precision Laboratories (APL) should be performed on plasma (blood in EDTA) serum collected during
the first two weeks after symptom onset.(8) DENV IgM antibodies are usually detectable 6 – 7 days after the onset of illness
and generally indicate a current or recent infection. A serum specimen should be collected for acute phase DENV IgM
antibody testing at least 5 days after of onset of clinical illness. A convalescent phase serum should be obtained at least 2
weeks after the first sample has been collected.
Antibody detection should be performed on whole blood in SST tubes.(9) Currently, the most frequently acquired flavivirus in
returning travelers is dengue. However, flavivirus antibodies can cross-react in the existing serological tests, especially those
for IgM, resulting in some uncertainty in IgM results between ZIKV and DENV. If ZIKV is suspected, cases with positive or
equivocal serology are sent to the National Microbiology Laboratory (NML) in Winnipeg to be confirmed using PRNT. This is
mainly indicated in scenarios where congenital ZIKV infection is suspected after consultation with the Virologist-on-call.
PRNT is not always able to provide a definitive determination of the specific flavivirus causing a recent infection, especially if
the person has a prior history of flavivirus infection (e.g., previous DENV or West Nile virus), is immunized against a flavivirus
(e.g., yellow fever or Japanese encephalitis) or is currently co-infected with another flavivirus. These cases may have test
results such as DENV IgM negative with positive PRNT results or positive PRNT results for both DENV and ZIKV, making it
difficult to determine which virus is causing the illness (i.e., probable flavivirus).
(Refer to the ProvLab website (specifically the Guide to Services) for the latest information regarding diagnosis and testing).
## Treatment
- There is no effective, specific therapy for DENV.(3)
- Symptomatic care should be made available if the case develops dehydration or severe disease.(3)
- Acetylsalicylic acid (ASA) and other non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are contraindicated as they may
aggravate the bleeding tendency associated with some DENV infections.(3,10)
- The risk of death can be reduced from 10% to 1% in severe cases if shock is recognized early and intensive
supportive therapies are initiated quickly.(10)
Reservoir
DENV is primarily maintained in a human - Aedes mosquito cycle.(3,11)
Populations of Aedes (aegypti or albopictus) mosquitoes are not established in Canada. The likelihood that Aedes mosquitos
will become established in Canada is considered very low as they are limited by the lack of environmental and climate
suitability here.(12)
## Transmission
DENV is mainly transmitted by the bite of the infected Aedes mosquitos.(3)
Although very rare, transmission may occur via needlestick, blood transfusion (especially in endemic countries), organ
transplant and vertically, especially if the mother is acutely ill around the time of delivery.(2,3,11,13)
The virus is sensitive to heat and is susceptible to many common disinfectants including ethanol, sodium hypochlorite, and
glutaraldehyde.(2) It is, however, stable in dried blood and exudates for up to 9 weeks at room temperature.(2,14)
## Incubation Period
The incubation period ranges from 3 – 14 days, but is typically 4 – 7 days.(3)
## Period of Communicability
DENV has a short period of communicability via blood, from shortly before until the end of the febrile period (usually 3 – 5
days).(3)

Host Susceptibility
Susceptibility is universal. Those living in or travelling to tropical areas are at highest risk of exposure to DENV. Those who
have been previously infected, babies and young children are more likely to get severe infection.(3)
Infection and recovery from one serotype was once thought to provide lifelong immunity to that serotype but no other
serotypes.(15) Recent evidence suggests re-infection with the same serotype is possible however, more research is
required.(16) Evidence also suggests that a subsequent infection by a different serotype increases the risk of acquiring DHF.(11)
Infants who were infected with DENV between 6 – 12 months of age, and born to previously DENV-infected mothers, are at
increased risk for severe disease.(3)
Incidence
DENV is not nationally notifiable. It has been notifiable in Alberta since 1985.(17) Between 2010 and 2015, less than 70 travel-
acquired cases were reported to Alberta Health. DENV is not considered endemic in Alberta. For more information on the
occurrence of dengue worldwide refer to the CDC Yellow Book webpage on dengue.

## Public Health Management
Key Investigation
- Confirm the diagnosis, if possible. Other arboviral diseases such as chikungunya, ZIKV or West Nile virus and rash
illnesses such as measles, rubella, parvovirus, enterovirus, or febrile illnesses such as malaria should be ruled out.(3) Co-
infection with multiple pathogens in returning travelers can occur.
- Determine where the case may have been exposed, taking into consideration the incubation period, reservoir, and mode
of transmission, including a history of:
- recent travel to an endemic area,
- recent emigration from an endemic area, or
- History of recent blood transfusion (within previous 21 days).
Management of a Case
- The following information should be provided to cases depending on their needs:
- DENV cases should defer donating blood for 56 days after the onset of symptoms; this may vary depending on the
country visited and length of time spent there. Refer to Canadian Blood Services website for more information.
- Provide education how to prevent transmission and infection (i.e., preventive measures below).
- Contact health care provider if symptoms worsen (refer to Clinical Presentation).
Management of Contacts
- There is no direct follow-up of contacts; however, it may be prudent to identify others who may have been exposed to the
same source as the case to find undiagnosed cases.
Preventative Measures
- Provide the following recommendations to persons travelling to endemic areas:
- Consult a health care provider or visit a travel health clinic preferably six weeks before travel.
- Protect yourself from mosquito bites:(18)
 remain in well-screened or completely enclosed, air-conditioned areas whenever possible,
 wear light-colored clothing and cover up as much as possible with long pants, long-sleeved loose fitting shirts,
shoes/boots (not sandals) and a hat.
 sleep under a bed net, preferably treated with insecticide, and
 use insect repellent (containing DEET or Icaridin/picardin), 15 minutes after sunscreen, on exposed skin and on
clothing.
- There is currently no DENV vaccine licensed for use in Canada.
