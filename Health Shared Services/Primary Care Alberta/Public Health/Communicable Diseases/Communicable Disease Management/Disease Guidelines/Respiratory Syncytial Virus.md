## Case Definition
## Confirmed Case
Clinical illness (A) with laboratory confirmation of infection:
- Detection of respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) nucleic acid (e.g., polymerase chain reaction [PCR]) in an appropriate
clinical specimen (e.g., nasopharyngeal swab or aspirate, throat swab(B), auger suction, bronchoalveolar lavage [BAL], or
bronchoalveolar wash)(C);
OR
- Detection of RSV viral antigen in nasopharyngeal cells by Immunofluorescence Assay (IFA) or Enzyme Immunoassay
(EIA);
OR
- Isolation of RSV from respiratory secretions in cell culture(D).
## Probable Case (Outbreak Only)
Clinical illness(A) in a person who is epidemiologically linked to a confirmed case.
(A) Primary infection manifests as pneumonia, bronchiolitis, tracheobronchitis or upper respiratory tract illness (often accompanied by fever and
otitis media). The infection is very rarely asymptomatic.
(B) Although throat swabs are not typically used in RSV diagnostic testing, a positive throat specimen would still be considered laboratory
confirmation of RSV infection.
(C) Refer to the Public Health Laboratory (ProvLab) Guide to Services for current specimen collection and submission information.
(D) Although culture is not routinely used for RSV diagnosis in Alberta, a positive RSV culture result would still be considered laboratory
confirmation of RSV infection(8).

## Reporting Requirements
Laboratories
- All laboratories shall report all positive RSV laboratory results by mail, fax, or electronic transfer within 48 hours (two
business days) to the Medical Officer of Health (MOH) (or designate) of the zone.
- All laboratories shall report all (positive and negative) RSV laboratory results by mail, fax, or electronic transfer within 48
hours (two business days) to the Chief Medical Officer of Health (CMOH) (or designate).
Alberta Health Services and First Nations and Inuit Health Branch
- The MOH (or designate) of the zone where the case currently resides shall report all confirmed RSV
hospitalizations/deaths (E) to the CMOH (or designate) via the COVID-19/Seasonal Influenza/RSV death and hospitalized
case report form using existing processes (e.g., CDOM or confidential fax).
− The report form must be submitted within one week of notification of hospitalization, discharge from hospital,
resolution of the RSV case status or death.(E).
- The MOH (or designate) shall report all RSV outbreaks to the CMOH (or designate) via the Alberta Outbreak Report Form
(AORF) as soon as possible using existing processes (e.g., CDOM or confidential fax).
− The initial report form must be submitted within 24 hours of opening the outbreak investigation and the final report
must be submitted within 48 hours of closing the outbreak investigation.
− Aggregate numbers must be reported weekly for outbreaks in continuing care and acute care settings while they
remain open.
- For out-of-province and out-of-country confirmed RSV cases that are reported as hospitalized/deceased, the MOH (or
designate) of the zone where the case resides shall forward the following information to the CMOH (or designate) using
existing processes (e.g., CDOM) within 48 hours (two business days):
− name,
− date of birth,
− out-of-province health care number,
− out-of-province address and phone number,
− positive laboratory report (if applicable), and
− other relevant clinical/epidemiological information.
(E) Confirmed RSV deaths: deaths where a lab-confirmed RSV infection is the direct or contributing cause that come to the attention of Public
Health, including deaths in the community.

Clinical Assessment and Epidemiology
## Etiology
RSV is an RNA virus of the Orthopneumovirus genus and the Pneumoviridae family.(1,2) Human RSV has two antigenic
subgroups, A and B, with both often circulating during the same RSV season.(2)
## Clinical Presentation
RSV is a common respiratory virus that typically causes mild cold-like symptoms that resolve within 1 to 2 weeks.(3,4)
Symptoms usually appear in stages and include rhinorrhea, coughing, sneezing, headache, fatigue, decrease in appetite, and
pharyngitis.(4,5) If bronchiolitis occurs, additional symptoms include wheezing, tachypnea, intercostal retractions, grunting and
nasal flaring; fever may or may not be present.(2)
RSV is common in childhood and is the most common cause of lower respiratory tract illness in young children.(5,6) Although
RSV most commonly presents as an upper respiratory tract infection, approximately 20-30% of infants develop lower
respiratory tract illness such as bronchiolitis, tracheobronchitis, or pneumonia with the first infection.(2,5) Infants with RSV may
present with non-specific signs and symptoms such as lethargy, difficulty breathing, difficulty feeding, and/or irritability,
tachypnea, and cyanosis.(1,5) Infants who develop RSV infection in the first weeks of life, especially preterm infants, are at high
risk of developing apnea.(2) Children under 5 may experience rapid breathing, trouble swallowing, or sepsis.(7)
Healthy adults and infants infected with RSV usually do not require hospitalization. Some older adults and/or infants younger
than 6 months of age may require hospitalization if they are having trouble breathing or are dehydrated; however, in most
cases hospitalization lasts a few days.(4) Approximately 1-3% of all children under age 1 will be hospitalized because of lower
respiratory tract infections caused by severe RSV, with the highest rate of RSV hospitalization occurring in the first six months
of life.(2)
## Diagnosis
Diagnosis of RSV is generally made through nucleic acid amplification tests (e.g., RT-PCR, LAMP).(2,8) Other diagnostic
assays such as direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) and enzyme or chromatographic immunoassays that detect viral antigen in
nasopharyngeal specimens have lower sensitivity.(2,8)
## Treatment
Treatment with antiviral medication is not routinely recommended and most infections resolve within 1 to 2 weeks.(4)
Supportive care is recommended for individuals with mild to moderate RSV infection.(5) Treatment for severe illness, such as
bronchiolitis in hospitalized infants, may include hydration, airway suction, and/or continuous positive airway pressure
(CPAP).(2)
Reservoir
Humans.(1,2)
## Transmission
RSV is spread through particles and droplets released into the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes, breathes or
speaks, or via other close contact such as kissing.(7) Indirect transmission may also occur by touching surfaces contaminated
with RSV and then touching eyes, nose or mouth.(2,7) RSV can survive on surfaces for several hours and on hands for 30
minutes or more.(2)

## Incubation Period
The incubation period for RSV ranges from two to eight days, with four to six days being the most common.(2)
## Period of Communicability
The period of communicability for RSV can begin one to two days prior to symptom onset. Viral shedding is typically three to
eight days.(2,4) In young infants and immunosuppressed children, viral shedding may last three to four weeks or longer.(2)
Host Susceptibility
Susceptibility is universal. Almost all children are infected by RSV at least once by age two and may experience reinfections
throughout life.(2,7) Subsequent infections can occur at any age as infection produces only partial and temporal immunity.(5)
However, subsequent infections are usually less severe.(2)
The risk of severe outcomes from RSV is higher among infants and children under two years of age, adults and children with
chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease or compromised immune systems, and adults over 65.(5)
Incidence
RSV infection is a seasonal illness; however, the onset, duration and peak varies annually. In the northern hemisphere, it
typically lasts from November to April, however it can be seen year-round in tropical and equatorial regions.(1)
For more information on global RSV surveillance, refer to the World Health Organization (WHO) RSV Surveillance Strategy.
For more information on seasonal RSV activity in Canada, refer to the Respiratory Virus Detection Surveillance website.
In Alberta, hospitalizations/deaths where a lab-confirmed RSV infection is the direct or contributing cause became reportable
in 2024. Alberta RSV data can be found on the Alberta respiratory virus dashboard website.

## Public Health Management (Outbreak Only)
Key Investigation
- Refer to the Management of RSV Outbreaks in Congregate Settings section.
- RSV outbreaks should be managed as per direction from the Zone MOH.
- The Zone MOH will determine the need and extent of outbreak control measures.
- For additional outbreak management measures in different settings refer to AHS guides for outbreak prevention and
## control.
Management of a Case
- Individuals with symptoms of respiratory illness should stay home. If symptoms worsen, they should seek medical
guidance or care.
- General guidance in high-risk settings should include infection prevention and control precautions to prevent disease
## transmission.
Management of Contacts
- Individual management of close contacts is NOT required and testing through the public health system is NOT indicated
for close contacts.

Preventative Measures
- Prevention is the most effective strategy to control RSV infections as treatment options are limited.
- Provide general and ongoing public education regarding preventative measures against respiratory illnesses including
RSV such as staying away from people who are sick, staying home if sick, covering coughs and sneezes (respiratory
etiquette), practicing frequent hand hygiene, and wearing a mask for 10 days after respiratory illness onset, while indoors
with other people.
- Some infants may be eligible to receive RSV immunoprophylaxis. Refer to the Alberta Pediatric RSV Prevention Program
for more information.
- For additional information on vaccines available in Alberta, refer to the Alberta Immunization Policy (AIP).

## Appendix: Management of Respiratory Illness Outbreaks
- In schools, child care settings and homeless shelters/temporary housing, the Respiratory Illness (RI) definition and the
Respiratory Illness Outbreak (RIO) definition will be used to identify, report and manage outbreaks that may be caused by
a variety of respiratory pathogens, including RSV.
Respiratory Illness (RI) Definition:
- NEW onset of TWO or more symptoms and at least one must be from List A:
− List A: cough, shortness of breath (SOB), sore throat, loss or altered sense of taste/smell, runny nose/nasal
congestion
− List B: fever, fatigue (significant and unusual), muscle ache/joint pain, headache, nausea/diarrhea
Respiratory Illness Outbreak (RIO) Definition
- Two or more cases with respiratory illness within 7 days with a common epidemiological link
AND
- NO respiratory pathogen identified OR one case of any respiratory pathogen identified (e.g., Influenza; COVID-19; RSV)
