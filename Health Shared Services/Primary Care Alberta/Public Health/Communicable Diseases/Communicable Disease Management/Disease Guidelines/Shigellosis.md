## Case Definition
## Confirmed Case
## Laboratory confirmation of infection with or without clinical illness(A):
- Isolation of Shigella species from an appropriate clinical specimen (e.g., sterile site, deep tissue wounds, stool, vomit or
urine)(B)
## Probable Case
Clinical illness(A) in a person who is epidemiologically linked to a confirmed case
(A) Clinical illness is characterized by diarrhea, fever, nausea and sometimes toxaemia, vomiting, cramps or tenesmus. Asymptomatic
infections may occur.
(B) Refer to the Public Health Laboratory (ProvLab) Guide to Services for current specimen collection and submission information.

## Reporting Requirements
Physicians, Health Practitioners and Others
Physicians, health practitioners and others shall notify the Medical Officer of Health (MOH) (or designate) of the zone, of all
confirmed and probable cases in the prescribed form by mail, fax or electronic transfer within 48 hours (two business days).
Laboratories
All laboratories shall report all positive laboratory results by mail, fax or electronic transfer within 48 hours (two business days)
to the:
- Chief Medical Officer of Health (CMOH) (or designate), and
- MOH (or designate) of the zone.
Alberta Health Services and First Nations Inuit Health Branch
- The MOH (or designate) of the zone where the case currently resides shall forward the initial Notifiable Disease Report
(NDR) of all confirmed and probable cases to the CMOH (or designate) within two weeks of notification and the final NDR
(amendment) within four weeks of notification.
- The MOH (or designate) shall report all outbreaks to CMOH (or designate) via the Alberta Outbreak Report Form (AORF)
as soon as possible using existing processes (e.g., CDOM or fax).
- For out-of-province and out-of-country reports, the following information should be forwarded to the CMOH (or designate)
by phone, fax or electronic transfer within 48 hours (two business days):
 name,
 date of birth,
 out-of-province health care number,
 out-of-province address and phone number,
 positive laboratory report, and
 other relevant clinical/epidemiological information.

## Epidemiology
## Etiology
Shigellosis is an acute bacterial disease caused by gram negative bacilli in the family Enterobacteriaceae.(1). It is the most
communicable of the bacterial diarrheas. Some strains produce Shigatoxin type 1 (Stx1) much like the verotoxin of E. coli
O157:H7.(2) Approximately 40 serotypes are divided into four groups depending on serologic similarity and fermentation
reactions:
Group A - Shigella dysenteriae
Group B - Shigella flexneri
Group C - Shigella boydii
Group D - Shigella sonnei
## Clinical Presentation
Shigellosis is unique among bacterial enteropathogens in that a very low dose of the organism readily produces disease in
humans. This may account for the high secondary attack rate in families. The severity of the presentation and case fatality
rates are functions of the host.
Shigellosis involves the large and distal small intestine.(3) Disease is most commonly characterized by diarrhea (may contain
blood and mucus or be watery) accompanied by fever, nausea, and tenesmus. It may be biphasic with an initial period of
watery diarrhea and cramps, followed by the development of dysentery (blood and mucus).(1,3) Febrile convulsions may be a
significant complication in young children. The illness is usually self-limited, lasting between one day and one month with an
average of seven days.(4) Some individuals have mild or asymptomatic infections. Bacteremia and pneumonia may occur but
are uncommon.
There is a rare fulminant form of bacillary dysentery secondary to a massive small intestine invasion by the bacteria. This may
be seen in children. Death early in the infection is common (the “Ikari” syndrome).(5)
Infection with S. dysenteriae is often associated with serious disease and more severe complications, including toxic
megacolon and hemolytic-uremic syndrome (HUS). It is one of the most common Shigella species to occur in developing
countries and least common in developed countries.
Some strains of S. flexneri can cause Reiter’s syndrome (reactive arthropathy) in persons who are genetically predisposed,
although Reiter’s syndrome can occur with any of the Shigella strains.(1,6–11) This is a common strain in developing countries
but also accounts for approximately one quarter of cases in the United States. It is now thought to be sexually transmitted.(1)
Shigella boydii is not a common isolate in North America but widespread in developing countries.
Infection with S. sonnei often results in a short clinical course. Infection may be fatal in immunocompromised individuals. This
is the most common strain in developed countries. It accounts for approximately 75% of cases in the US.
## Diagnosis
Diagnosis is made by the isolation of Shigella from feces or a rectal swab or detection of Shigella nucleic acid by PCR.(1,3). The
infection is generally associated with a high number of leukocytes (pus cells) in the fecal matter.
Shigella remains viable outside the human body for only a short period of time. Hence, specimens must be processed rapidly
after collection, preferable within 24 hours. Use of appropriate media increases the likelihood of organism isolation. (12)
Serological testing is not generally helpful.

## Treatment
- Treatment is recommended for most symptomatic patients. Use of antibiotics will shorten the period of fecal excretion of
the infecting strain and will shorten the clinical course of disease often to a few days and may be lifesaving in the event
the case has S. dysenteriae.(4)
 Antibiotic resistance frequently develops after treatment.
- Replace fluids and electrolytes if there is excessive fluid loss through diarrhea or vomiting.
- Antimotility agents are not recommended, as they may prolong the course of disease or play a role in the development of
toxic dilation of the colon or perforation.(4)
Reservoir
Humans and primates are the reservoir for shigella.(1,3) The source is most often the feces of infected individuals who have
diarrhea. Other potential sources include contaminated food (especially when unrefrigerated, e.g., salads), raw vegetables,
milk, dairy products, and water.(1) Outbreaks in primates such as monkeys and chimpanzees have occurred.
## Transmission
The primary mode of transmission is fecal-oral from an infected person (symptomatic or asymptomatic): improper hand
hygiene, lack of sanitary conditions, overcrowding, eating or drinking contaminated food or water and sexual contact are risk
factors for transmitting infection.(1,3) Direct transmission is common in children and individuals who do not thoroughly clean
their hands and under the fingernails following defecation.
## Incubation Period
Generally, the incubation period for Shigella is usually 1–3 days but can range from 12–96 hours (4 days).(1,3) The incubation
period for S. dysenteriae can be longer, up to one week.
## Period of Communicability
Shigellosis can be spread while the person has acute infection and until the organism is no longer present in feces.(1,3,4) This
can last as long as four weeks. Long-term carriage is uncommon; however, asymptomatic carriers do have the ability to
transmit disease. The duration of carriage may be reduced to a few days with the use of appropriate antibiotics.
Host Susceptibility
The individuals at highest risk of acquiring shigellosis include:
- children ≤ 5 years of age in child care settings and their caregivers,
- the elderly,
- people living in crowded conditions,
- individuals with HIV,
- men who have sex with men, and
- debilitated and malnourished individuals are at increased risk of infection.(1–3,13)
Travellers to developing countries may be more at risk of acquiring antibiotic-resistant strains of Shigella.(14–17)
Secondary attack rates can be as high as 40% in households.(1) Breastfeeding is protective for infants.(3) Serotype-specific
immunity can be demonstrated in those who have recovered.(18)

Incidence
Shigellosis occurs worldwide.(18) Up to 15% of all diarrhea cases can be linked to Shigella infections.(2) It is estimated that up to
165 million cases and up to 1 million deaths due to Shigella occur worldwide each year.(19,20) Multi-antibiotic resistant Shigella
strain outbreaks have appeared worldwide. Children under 5 years of age account for the highest rate of cases (69%) and the
highest death rate (61%).(20) Secondary attack rates in households have been shown to be as high as 40%.(1) S. dysenteriae is
more commonly reported in developing countries while S. sonnei is more commonly reported in developed countries.(1) S.
boydii is found almost exclusively on the Indian subcontinent.(21)
Shigellosis is notifiable both in Alberta and Canada. The most common serotype in Alberta is S. sonnei. Outbreaks of
shigellosis have been reported in Alberta. Sources are often difficult to identify but over-crowding is often a factor in on-going
transmission. Large urban centers report the highest number of cases. Shigellosis is most prevalent in young children under
the age of 10.(22)
For more information of shigellosis cases in Alberta, refer to Interactive Health Data Application.

## Public Health Management
Key Investigation
- Confirm the diagnosis.
- Obtain a history of illness including the date of onset, and signs and symptoms.
- Identify any underlying medical conditions that may increase host susceptibility.
- Determine the occupation of the case and if in a sensitive situation or occupation (SSO) that poses a higher risk of
## transmission to others. See Table 1 for a definition of SSO.
- Determine the possible source of infection, taking into consideration the incubation period(C), reservoir, and mode of
## transmission. Assessment may include determining, obtaining or identifying:
 a detailed food history,
 history of institutionalization,
 contact with sewage contaminated recreational water,
 consumption of untreated surface water,
 high risk sexual practices, especially contact with feces,
 international travel or recent immigration (especially to/from areas with inadequate sanitation, water and sewage
## treatment), and
 residing in areas with poor sanitation including improper water treatment and sewage disposal.
- Assess for history of similar symptoms in other members of the household.
- Suspected contaminated food may be held or destroyed to prevent consumption.
- Identify any contacts, especially those in SSO that pose a higher risk of transmission to others – Refer to Table 1.
Contacts include:
 persons living in the household,
 children and child care workers at a child care facility (daycare, dayhome, or other child care site), and
 individuals exposed to the same source where the source is identified.

Management of a Case
- Antibiotics are often recommended for public health control.
- All cases should be advised of the following:
how the disease is transmitted, appropriate personal hygiene, routine infection prevention and control practices, and
contact precautions,
 to avoid food preparation until symptoms have resolved, and
 to avoid public recreational water (e.g., swimming pools) until after treatment is completed and diarrhea has
resolved.(3)
- Contact precautions should be used in health care settings where children or adults have poor hygiene or incontinence
that cannot be contained. Otherwise, routine practices are adequate.

Preventive Measures
- Educate the public about the following:
personal hygiene, especially the sanitary disposal of items containing feces,
careful hand washing after defecation and sexual contact, and before preparing or eating food,
washing cutting boards, counter tops and utensils with soap and water after contact with raw meat,
cooking meats thoroughly,
methods of personal protective measures, in particular the correct and consistent use of condoms and discuss risk of
sexual practices that permit fecal-oral contact,
accessing and drinking safe water supplies, and
testing private water supplies for presence of parasitic contamination, if suspected.
- Educate food handlers about:
 proper food and equipment handling, preparation, and hygiene, especially in avoiding cross-contamination from raw
meat products, and
 thorough hand washing before and after meal preparation.
