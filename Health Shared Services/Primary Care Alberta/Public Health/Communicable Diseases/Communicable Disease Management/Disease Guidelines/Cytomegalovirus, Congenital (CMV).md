## Case Definition
## Confirmed Case
Laboratory confirmation of cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection in a clinical specimen collected within the first three weeks of life
with or without clinical illness(A):
- Detection of CMV virus from an appropriate clinical specimen(B) by molecular diagnostic techniques (i.e. quantitative
PCR),
OR
- Histopathological evidence of CMV inclusion disease from an appropriate clinical specimen(B).
## Probable Case
Clinical illness (A) in a child/infant of any age, born to a CMV seropositive childbearing parent.
(A) See Clinical presentation
(B) Refer to the Public Health Laboratory (ProvLab) Guide to Services for current specimen collection and submission information.

## Reporting Requirements
Physicians, Health Practitioners and Others
Physicians, health practitioners and others shall notify the Zone Medical Officer of Health (MOH) (or designate), of all
confirmed and probable cases in the prescribed form by mail, fax or electronic submission within 48 hours (two business
days).
Laboratories
All laboratories shall report all positive laboratory results by mail, fax or electronic submission within 48 hours (two business
days) to the:
- Chief Medical Officer of Health (CMOH) (or designate), and
- Zone MOH (or designate).
Alberta Health Services and First Nations Inuit Health Branch
- The Zone MOH (or designate) where the case currently resides shall forward the Perinatally Acquired Notifiable Disease
Enhanced Report form for all confirmed and probable congenital cases to the CMOH (or designate) within four weeks of
## notification.
- For out-of-province and out-of-country cases, the Zone MOH should forward the following information to the CMOH (or
designate) by phone, fax or electronic submission within 48 hours (two business days):
name,
date of birth,
out-of-province health care number,
out-of-province address and phone number,
positive laboratory report, and
other relevant clinical/epidemiological information.

Clinical Assessment and Epidemiology
## Etiology
Cytomegalovirus (CMV) is a slowly replicating DNA virus. It is a member of the Herpesvirus group (herpesvirus 5).(1–3)
## Clinical Presentation
Approximately 1% of all newborns are perinatally infected with CMV, making it the most common congenital infection.(3–5) For
those born with CMV, approximately 10–15% of newborns demonstrate symptoms of infection including, intrauterine growth
restriction, neonatal jaundice, purpura, hepatosplenomegaly, microcephaly, brain damage, intracerebral calcifications, and
retinitis.(3,6) However, as many as 6–25% of those born infected but asymptomatic can develop complications including
permanent sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) and psychomotor mental delay.(7,8) Death may occur in utero.(1,3) Post-natal
morbidity occurs in 30–40% of symptomatic cases.(6)
## Diagnosis
The diagnosis of confirmed cases is by detection of the virus from an appropriate clinical specimen.(B)(8,9)
## Treatment
- Supportive treatment.
- There is no cure for congenital CMV although treatment with antivirals could offer some hearing improvement and reduce
developmental delays if used during the first year of life for symptomatic cases.(1–3)
Reservoir
Humans.(3,10)
## Transmission
The fetus may be infected in utero from either a primary or reactivated parental infection with the former having 10-15 times
higher transmission rates.(1,8) Congenital infections occurs by vertical transmission of CMV to fetus in utero by transplacental
passage of parental virus.(6,10) Children born with CMV may shed virus for five to six years and have potential to infect
others.(3)
## Incubation Period
Incubation period varies with the route of infection (i.e., congenital vs perinatal) and the size of inoculum.(9)
## Period of Communicability
The virus is excreted in the urine and saliva of newborns for many months. The excretion may persist or be episodic for
several years following a primary infection, possibly five or six years following a neonatal infection. Those infected with CMV
generally begin shedding and can transmit virus approximately one month after becoming infected.(3)

Host Susceptibility
The virus is ubiquitous, thus infection is present in all populations, with sero-prevalence increasing with age and decreasing in
developed countries.(3,9,11) Fetuses; individuals with debilitating diseases, immunodeficiency or immunosuppression; organ
recipients and people living with untreated HIV are more susceptible to disease.(3) Antibodies develop with infection, but are
insufficient to confer immunity, and reactivation can occur after primary infection.(6,11)
Incidence
Congenital CMV is not notifiable nationally. However, Canadian incidence has been estimated to affect 0.5% to 1% of live
births.(1,6,12) Congenital CMV became notifiable in Alberta in 1985. Refer to the Interactive Health Data Application (IHDA) for
more information on disease incidence in Alberta.

## Public Health Management
Key Investigation
- Assess whether infant meets case definition for confirmed or probable congenital infection.
- Determine current status of the infant, including presence/absence of symptoms.
- Identify if disease or antibodies are present in birth parent.
Management of a Case
- Supportive.
- For hospitalized cases, follow recommended hospital infection prevention and control precautions.
- Recommend infected infants receive close medical follow up by a pediatrician to monitor for hearing developmental
delays which would benefit from intervention.(1,2,4)
- Additional investigations (bloodwork and imaging) may be warranted to identify evidence of other end organ disease.(5)
Management of Contacts
- Due to the high prevalence of asymptomatic shedders in the population, there is no follow up of contacts.
Preventive Measures
- Pregnant persons should avoid sharing food and utensils/cups with children.(4,10,13)
- Persons of childbearing potential that work in the following occupational settings should follow routine infection prevention
and control practices.
hospitals, particularly labour and delivery and pediatric units.
daycare centres, with preschoolers or with persons unable to maintain their own personal hygiene.
▪ Strict hand hygiene after contact is vital (notably after changing diapers or assisting with toileting)
▪ Risk seems to be greatest when caring for children less than two years of age.(4,10,14)
