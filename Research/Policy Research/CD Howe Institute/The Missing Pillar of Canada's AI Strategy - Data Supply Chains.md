This article discusses the wealth of data that is available from governments - specifically from Statistics Canada that could be useful in training large langusage models.  Making this data available to commercial organizations does expose citizen data that even when an approach is taken to de-identify the data. Evidence shows that when taking data sets from say the health system, that correlating that data with other data sets, like high profile athletes where injury events and treatment are know, allow for the citizen data to be linked together.

Anindya Sen proposes a model whereby non-governmental organizations (NGO) are able to request access to a candidate data set.  Similar to a research ethics board, that request would evaluate the risk of harm to individuals balanced by the benefit to society all taken into consideration with the marginal cost of creating the data set.  The cost is proposed to be born by the NGO.

A key issue is that currently, individuals rarely have the opportunity to opt out of having their personal data shared.  Examples from telcos and information technology organizations like Google show the power of having massive data sets available for analysis.
