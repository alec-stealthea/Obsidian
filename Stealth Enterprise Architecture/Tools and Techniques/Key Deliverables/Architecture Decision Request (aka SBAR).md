Every spy knows you don't hand headquarters a 47-page dossier when they need to make a call in the next five minutes. The SBAR format is your one-page mission brief — just enough intel to enable a decision, no more, no less.

The Architecture Alternatives deliverable is probably the most frequently deployed weapon in your arsenal for providing information to decision makers at all levels. It borrows from clinical medicine, where practitioners need to communicate concisely in urgent situations and during patient handoffs. We've adopted this situational briefing model using the acronym SBAR:

- **S**ituation — a concise statement of the problem
- **B**ackground — relevant context (and only relevant context)
- **A**ssessment — analysis and consideration of the options
- **R**ecommendation — what you're asking them to decide

> [!tip] Lead with the Recommendation. While SBAR puts the recommendation at the end, Stealth EA practitioners know better than to bury the lead. Consider using **RSBA** order — Recommendation first — especially for executive audiences. Tell them what you think they should do, then back it up.

## The Approach

A recommendation typically needs to be completed in days, not months. Perfectionism is the enemy of progress here. Adopt an iterative approach where you create a complete (if rough) work product daily. Like any good field report, get something on paper that could brief the Chief today, then refine it tomorrow.

For larger scope alternatives, you may need a formal project or procurement activity. This deliverable is intended to resolve conflicts where multiple existing alternatives compete for a particular situation. Where you expect many decisions over time on related facets, a Working Group may form — and they'll create more than just this single deliverable.

When alternatives have been reviewed and assessment agreed upon, make the recommendation. Then it goes to the appropriate decision-making body for review and approval.
## The Team

Three teams typically contribute to an Alternatives Assessment:

- **Recommendation Team** — Your core operatives. This working group brings together the perspectives needed to see the full picture. They set context using a [[Solution Concept Model]] and/or Initiative Summary or Program Plan, then isolate the specific concern requiring a decision.
- **Extended Team** — Your network of informants. Subject Matter Experts and affected stakeholders who get interviewed for requirements clarification and implications assessment. They provide the ground truth you need.
- **Governance Team** — Remember: the SBAR is only a recommendation. The decision always lies with the stakeholders impacted by the outcome. Your mission as the Stealth EA is to resist the temptation to say "I told you so" when they don't move forward with your recommendation. (At least, not out loud. And not immediately.)
## What's in the SBAR?

The SBAR deliverable formally documents a recommendation for an appropriate governance body. This could be anything from a scope change affecting an initiative, selection of one application over another, or a recommended process change within a domain. The key to a good SBAR is just enough information to enable the decision — like any good field report, give them exactly what they need to greenlight the mission or abort it.
### Recommendation Overview

While the recommendation appears at the end in the SBAR acronym, don't bury the lead. State your recommendation up front along with the key implications and risks. Busy executives will thank you.
### Situation
Why is this analysis needed now? Why is this decision being brought to this particular governance body? Set the urgency and relevance immediately.
### Background
Provide relevant context — no more, no less. In a project scenario, start with the Initiative Summary to shape the context and use that picture to write the narrative. Resist the urge to include everything you learned; include only what the decision-makers need.
### Assessment
Present your alternatives. You can have as few as two and as many as needed, but keep the total assessment to two pages maximum. For each alternative, describe it clearly and provide three pros and three cons.

You'll likely create several models during assessment, though typically the [[Solution Concept Model]] is the centrepiece — possibly one variant for each major alternative.

- **The Do Nothing Alternative** — This isn't laziness; it's tradecraft. The "do nothing" option smokes out the real requirements and forces stakeholders to articulate why action matters. It also highlights risks of inaction. This option may not survive to the final briefing, but it's essential reconnaissance during your first iteration.
- **The Silver Bullet** — Usually there's a leading contender. The answer everyone thinks is correct. Use this alternative to ensure context is set appropriately and assumptions are tested. Sometimes the obvious answer is right. Sometimes it isn't.
- **Gold/Bronze Alternatives** — These are typically more expensive/inclusive options (Gold) or less expensive options that don't fully meet requirements (Bronze). You may include both and remove the Do Nothing option in your final version.
### Recommendation

Expand on your opening recommendation with full rationale. Explain why you're recommending this alternative over the others. Be direct. Be clear. Be confident — but acknowledge uncertainty where it exists.
### Participants

Include everyone consulted during the analysis, along with their role title for the context of this work (which may differ from their usual title). This provides transparency and allows decision-makers to assess whether the right perspectives were included.

## Guarding Against Bias

Every operative has blind spots. Before finalizing your recommendation:

- Seek peer review, especially from someone likely to disagree with your conclusion
- Check whether you gathered evidence to support a predetermined conclusion ([[Decision-based Evidence Making]])
- Ensure the "Do Nothing" alternative received genuine consideration
- Verify that pros and cons are balanced in their depth and rigour across all alternatives

---

_Remember: The best SBAR is one that gets read and acted upon. Keep it tight, keep it clear, and trust your governance team to make the call. Your job is to give them what they need — not to make the decision for them._
