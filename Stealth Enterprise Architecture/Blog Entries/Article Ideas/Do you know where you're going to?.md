How many times when there's a new challenge or opportunity is the suggestion to do a current state analysis? Let me be clear. I am not suggesting that there's anything wrong with a current state analysis. It's just that unless you know where you're going, you don't know where to look.

If you map out all the streets in the city, but realize that you're wanting to travel internationally, you really only needed to map the ways to get to the airport.
## Start with the Destination

So start first with what the destination is for the particular solution. How do you imagine the future? Think of it like casting a movie – you need to know the story before you can pick your actors.

**Identify Your Cast** - Like any solution, start with the cast. This includes active actors as well as the stakeholders who need to interact within the solution space. Don't forget the skills and capabilities of your back-of-house staff that need to implement whatever you're cooking up. Are they seasoned performers or first-day extras? This matters more than you think.

**What is your stage?** - Is your scope an entire domain, or just one value stream. Just one business capability?

**Pick Your Timeline** - Next, select a timeframe. The Stealth EA crystal ball dims greatly after three years, but most people seem to like a five-year timeframe. It's like planning a vacation – too short and you're stressed, too long and - well - what's too long for a vacation?

**Paint the Possible Futures** - What in any version of the future is going to happen? Now think about an ideal world for the domain you're solutioning in. What would make your stakeholders more successful? What would have them telling success stories at industry conferences?
## The Reverse Journey

Now with the end in mind, look back at current state. It's like using GPS in reverse – instead of "turn left in 500 meters," you're asking "what roads could have gotten me here from there?"

Think about value and quick iterations that move you down the path to get you to the final destination. Don't put in a timeframe, just the sequence of events and dependencies that take you to your destination. That said, most organizations think in one year fiscal planning cycles. While generating a return on investment within a year is difficult, thinking in value generated within the financial planning cycle 

This isn't about creating a detailed project plan with Gantt charts that would make a project manager weep tears of joy. This is about understanding the logical stepping stones to get through to the destination.
## Why This Matters

When you start with destination-first thinking, something magical happens. Your current state analysis becomes surgical rather than exploratory. You're not documenting everything that exists – you're documenting everything that matters for the journey you're planning to take.

It's the difference between packing for "a trip somewhere" versus packing for "three days in London in November." One approach leaves you with seventeen different shoes and no umbrella. The other gets you exactly what you need.
## The Bottom Line

Before you dive into mapping every process, cataloging every system, and interviewing every stakeholder about their current pain points, pause and ask: "Where exactly are we trying to go?" Because if you don't know your destination, any current state analysis you do is just expensive tourism of your own organization.
