After more than a couple of decades in some form of Enterprise Architecture, I am left to ask the following question. Is it time to see if we can stop having more than one architect talking, becoming a discussion that requires Queensbury Rules? While spirited debates can sharpen our understanding, much of the discourse amongst architects online tends to be more self-serving than constructive. As someone who believes EA is an enabler, I offer a take on three common points of contention in our field:

1. **"One Framework to Rule Them All"** Every time I log in, there's another epic battle about which EA framework reigns supreme. TOGAF vs. Zachman vs. [Insert New Framework Here]. It's like watching a debate team in action but with more acronyms. While frameworks are essential, remember: the best framework is the one that works for your organization and actually helps people meet their goals. So, let's stop the methodology wars and create something useful for all your stakeholders.
    
2. **The Tool/Language Tussle :** "Which EA tool is the best?" is a question that sparks endless debates and, frankly, some pretty intense PowerPoint presentations. While these discussions can be enlightening, they often turn into a showdown of features, integrations, and buzzwords. Newsflash: the best tool is the one your team will actually use and find beneficial. Instead of trying to out-tech each other, let’s focus on finding solutions that make our colleagues’ lives easier. Remember, there are aspects of Enterprise Architecture for almost any role within the organization. The tools are not just for enterprise architects.
    
3. **The Ivory Tower Syndrome:** We’ve all encountered the "Ivory Tower" architect, who speaks in complex diagrams and jargon that would make a sci-fi author blush. Even worse is when this ivory tower architect expands to become an Architecture Review Board looking to enforce standards.  EA should demystify and streamline complexity, not add to it. Let's aim to be the architects who build bridges between business and IT, not just lofty towers of theory and barriers to innovation. Instead of an ARB, how about a peer review process to support the other architects in developing useful deliverables?
    
Enterprise Architecture should be about making other people more successful. It's not about winning debates - especially within our own profession. While many will write about how visible and high up the Enterprise Architecture function should report, I am a larger fan of stealth enterprise architecture. You can be stealth by taking these actions:
- Apply what works best for where you and your organization is at.  
- Use the tools you have at your disposal. 
- Make sure you stay relevant to your stakeholders. 

And finally, maybe don't argue quite so much.
