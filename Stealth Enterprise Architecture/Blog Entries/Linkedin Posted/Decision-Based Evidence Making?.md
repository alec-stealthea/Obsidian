How un-biased are you with the recommendations you make? 

The most common enterprise architecture deliverable I create is the Architecture Decision Request. If not on a weekly basis, it's pretty close to that. While I think we all look to present the fairest assessment possible, there's many times that does not turn out to be the case.

A prevalent yet precarious approach in this journey is 'Decision-Based Evidence Making. In healthcare we would espouse the term evidence-based decision making in the way that we determine a patient's course of action. Too often in architecture, we skew the evidence to support the decision that is desired within the organization or within ourselves.

Sometimes the author is to blame. Personal bias perspectives include:

1. **"I Love This Tech!" Syndrome**: I love new tech! We sometimes get starry-eyed over certain technologies. It's like a tech crush, and who listens to reason when in love? I mean, this ‘[[Stealth Enterprise Architecture/Blog Entries/Linkedin Posted/2024 Buzzword Bingo]]’ technology is for sure the hammer for all problems.
2. **The Need for Speed**: Quick decisions can be thrilling - like speed dating with information technology. In fact, time-boxing is a great tactic to avoid analysis paralysis. 
3. **Echo Chambers R Us**: We all love to be right. So, we often listen to echoes of our own thoughts rather than seeking diverse opinions.

Other times there’s pressure that may be outside of our control. Some common causes of this.

1. **Forensic Evidence** - an incident happened and now leaders or external stakeholder want to know some form of root cause or the rationale for a particular decision. 
2. **Decision Maker Prejudice** - when there's only one answer in the eyes of the person signing off
3. **Don’t rock the boat** - just going along to get along.
4. **Architecture Standards** - I have seen many an Architecture Review Board discount viable and realistic options rigorously adhering to standards. 

There is skill required in writing an architecture decision request. A formula used in healthcare is SBAR - Situation, Background, Assessment, and Recommendation. Many times I think it should be RSBA where the recommendation comes first as everyone (usually) just wants to know the answer. It's in the Analysis that prejudice shows up.  In one SBAR I reviewed the author was quite transparent. In the Pro and Con table, the cons for options two & three was they they were not option one. 

To try and combat decision-based evidence making, I always include the option of doing nothing as sometimes, that's the best thing to do. It also usually helps writing how the alternatives are better than doing nothing. After that, I usually also only include two other options in the final report, even if there were many avenues explored. After you have written your SBAR, it's usually good to have it peer reviewed and, if possible, by someone who you know may not prefer the alternative you are recommending.

It is worth investing time in the skills required to create compelling architecture decision requests. Being known to write a clearly and have demonstrated an unbiased assessment approach is usually appreciated.  In my experience decision makers will want to see more of your work since it will allow them to be more successful.
