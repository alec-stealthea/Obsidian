In the world of enterprise architecture (EA), we often find ourselves striving for perfection in modelling our organizations. Statistician George Box remind us of a crucial lesson: "All models are wrong, but some are useful." This quote encapsulates a profound truth that is particularly relevant in the dynamic and complex field of enterprise architecture.
### Models in Enterprise Architecture
Models in EA are simplified representations of a complex reality. They are tools that help us understand, analyze, and plan the structure and operations of an organization. However, no model can capture the entirety of an organization's intricacies, uncertainties, and evolving nature. This is where Box's wisdom becomes invaluable.
### Embrace Imperfection
Acknowledging that all models are inherently flawed does not diminish their value. Instead, it encourages a pragmatic approach to enterprise architecture. By accepting imperfection, architects can focus on what makes a model useful: its ability to provide insights, guide decision-making, and drive meaningful changes.

Imperfections allow architects to engage with their audience as they point out the errors of your model. While not encouraging intentional errors, a better lesson learned is that a model that has not been shared is not getting the opportunity to be useful. A good enough model shared early will become more useful, faster.

### Criteria for Usefulness
For a model to be useful in enterprise architecture, it should:

1. **Address a Specific Audience**: There is forever the view that there is the all singing, all dancing, singular model. That will never hold true. Create the model that speaks to your audience and conveys the messages that they want to hear.
2. **Be Understandable**: It should be easily comprehensible to stakeholders, facilitating communication and collaboration. Use as plain a language as possible. Label all the components - especially lines that connect objects.
3. **Be useful:** The ultimate test of a model’s utility is its capacity to inform and inspire effective action. Sometimes the action is making a decision. For others it's providing guidance on what's next. For others it's reassurance that there is a design or plan in place.

**Are your models useful?**

Take another look at your models. Are they as useful as they could be?
