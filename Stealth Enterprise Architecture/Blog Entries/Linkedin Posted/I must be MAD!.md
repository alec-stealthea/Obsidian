In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, the ability to streamline an organisation's value streams and enhance decision-making is more crucial than ever. A key asset in supporting any organisation is developing and maintaining master architecture data (MAD). This is where the architects take the key organisational design data entities and use master data management techniques to be able to cross-map these entities across the organisation. While this is a first and crucial step, of more value is building relationships between the entities themselves and how they may change over time.

### What is MAD?

One of the larger uses for master data management is uniquely identifying individuals like customers. For other enterprise architecture practices the focus is largely on the technology platforms. To be MAD, the focus is more on business architecture and how those entities and relationships change over time.

- **Application Catalogue** - In a digital world, applications are usually key tools to accelerate and optimise value streams. While the configuration management database (CMDB) is an application of record for an organisation's application portfolio, it is (usually) incomplete. Further, it may be designed more to support the technology that enables applications than the application design itself.
- **Business Domains** - Breaking an organisation into domains provides the ability to break the organisation into areas for focus. This is usually hierarchical and while influenced by the organisational structure, it should resist organisational change. This entity is where the MAD curators and MAD is the authoritative source.
- **Business Capability Catalogue** - This is another MAD entity curated and managed in MAD. Curating and governing the business capability catalogue forms the basis for business capability planning using value streams.
- **Locations** - For any large organisation the ability to curate a location inventory and build the relationships between many other MAD entities. Locations can span many additional concepts in the realm of geospatial intelligence.
- **Organisations** - No organisation is an island. They exist within an ecosystem of suppliers, regulators, partners, and many other stakeholders who are essential for daily operations and planning into the future.
- **MAD Relationships** - Of all of the MAD constructs, the relationships between MAD entities can not be treated as simple relationships. The relationships themselves have data associated with them that is essential in making MAD an effective resource.
- **Roles** - Managing key roles within an organisation can be quite difficult. The human resources department usually manages roles from a compensation perspective. There are also legally governed roles, roles based on the organisation, and roles defined in the financial hierarchy. Finally, IT usually also looks to implement some form of role-based access. Curating roles from these suppliers provides an effective tool for mapping relationships with other MAD entities.
- **Services** - An organisation's products and services to its customers are one of the most complicated MAD entities. Branding and re-branding or services over time with sometimes different naming for different market segments or geographies can be difficult to stay on top of.
- **MAD Time** - After relationships between entities, adding the time dimension is critical to support planning. This can be when a new location is being built or sold, an application's lifecycle. The MAD time scale is usually measured in months vs. smaller increments. The recommendation is to start with calendar or fiscal quarters (three-month increments).
### Why invest in MAD?

1. **Enabling Strategic Decision-Making** - Master architecture data is the backbone of strategic planning, providing a clear and coherent view of an organisation's design and interdependencies. By maintaining up-to-date master architecture data, companies can ensure that their IT strategy aligns with their business objectives, facilitating more informed decision-making. This alignment is particularly crucial in times of change, such as during mergers, acquisitions, or market expansions, where a comprehensive understanding of the architectural landscape can significantly smooth transitions.
2. **Enhancing Organisational Agility** - The agility of a business is measured by how quickly it can respond to market changes and internal demands. Master architecture data enhances this agility by offering a consolidated view across multiple architectural domains. This integration enables businesses to identify potential synergies, streamline processes, and deploy new technologies that allow value streams to perform more efficiently.
3. **Improving Risk and Issue Management** - Risk management is a critical concern for any business, and master architecture data plays a pivotal role in mitigating IT-related risks. With MAD, organisations can better understand the potential impacts of system failures and data breaches. This understanding allows for the development of more effective contingency and recovery plans. Additionally, master architecture data aids in compliance management by ensuring that all systems adhere to relevant standards and regulations, thereby reducing legal and financial risks.
### Conclusion

Creating and maintaining master architecture data is not a back office data custodian issue. The tools, skills, and processes that build the MAD infrastructure support strategic business initiatives that can significantly influence an organisation's adaptability, efficiency, and competitive standing. As businesses continue to navigate digital transformation, the role of master architecture data will only grow, proving essential for those looking to thrive in a digital-first world.

For businesses looking to implement or enhance their master architecture data, the journey involves continuous refinement and alignment with business goals. However, the benefits outlined above demonstrate that this effort is essential for sustainable success in today’s fast-paced business environment.

My advice. Start with one supplier for each entity and build out and expose MAD to as much of the organisation as possible. The second best next step is to make your MAD portal a supplier to enable crowd-sourced information - especially building entity relationships since suppliers of this information may be more difficult to surface from data sources.
