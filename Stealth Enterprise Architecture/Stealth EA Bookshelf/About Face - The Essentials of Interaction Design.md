Enterprise architects build models full of applications, data flows, and business capabilities. But somewhere between the swim lanes and the sequence diagrams, the people disappear. Alan Cooper’s About Face offers a correction: personas as a rigorous method for keeping human goals at the centre of architectural decisions.

About Face was published by Alan Cooper in 1995. Since then, we have the advent of whole new user experience paradigms that did not yet exist. The principles of how people interact with devices, especially technology devices remains sound. Cooper coined the term “persona” and pioneered goal-directed design.

One of the goals of stealth enterprise architecture is to help other people be more successful. While this is a generally good approach broadly, it is particularly important in the design work that enterprise architects do on a daily basis. To that end, a Stealth EA guideline is that models, no matter the perspective, should have people in them.  

Why? It’s the point of the solution. Without people, what’s the point of the design?  Too often architects get lost in the complexity of the perspective they are designing, especially as you move down to the information and technology architecture levels of design.  This is evident when there’s no people in the models they create.  

But let’s take that down another level.  Let’s say you have included a business actor or stakeholder in your model.  How do you know how they should influence your design? [[Building a Domain Persona]] is a way of understanding way that person wants to achieve and why. 

A persona is an abstraction of either a role, a group of roles, or possibly, a particular segment of actors within a role. Personas present as individuals but intentionally represent groups. Cooper insists personas emerge from rigorous research, not assumptions. The goal: synthesis of actual user patterns rather than stereotypes built on perception.

Key to the persona is understanding goals. This is an extension of the books main thesis which is goal-directed design. These goals are broken down into three types of goals:
- **Experience goals** - Goals that are typically specific to the application or applications that are within the scope of the development of the persona. Cooper recommends a Goldilocks theory of user design. Don't design for the novice or the expert, design for the goals of the intermediate user. These could manifest in the acceptance criteria for user stories as part of a business capability instance definition.
- **End Goals** - Motivations for a specific task within a process. For the Stealth EA, this can be illustrated either within a specific capability instance within a [[Value Streams|value stage]] stage, or more abstractly using a [[User Journey Maps|journey map]] across one or more value stages of the value stream. If the persona being constructed happens to be the stakeholder receiving value from the value stream, then the goals become a foundational part of the motivation aspects of the value stream.
- **Life Goals** - Go beyond the context of the application or application usage context.  It is difficult to create a meaningful connection between a technology device or application and someone's life goals. If you do, you end up with a loyal user.

User goals are the foundation and a key aspect of building a user persona. Many times the enterprise architect and business analyst needs to understand the organization's goals and objectives to determine if there's a way to align the two. The stakeholder persona can be equally compelling, though maybe not as robust since there’s not the same user population to sample as may be available for user personas.

If as a Stealth Enterprise Architect you have not been exposed to the science of user experience design, I would highly recommend About Face. Using the technique of creating personas will enrich your understanding of how user needs intersect with the different domains of enterprise architecture. At over six hundred pages, it's a daunting yet comprehensive design thinking for the paradigms of the time.  Today with voice input, touch screens, and soon spatial awareness there could be much more added. The first two sections for Goal-oriented design and Designing Behaviour and Form are timeless.
