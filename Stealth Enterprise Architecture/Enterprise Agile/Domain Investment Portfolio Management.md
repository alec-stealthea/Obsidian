## Why domains matter for portfolio management

Before we dive into portfolio management mechanics, we need to talk about who actually owns these portfolios. The answer isn't "the PMO" or "the CIO's office" or "that committee nobody remembers creating." The answer is: **business domains**.

Here's the uncomfortable truth most organizations discover too late: if you organize your portfolio management around your org chart, you'll be reorganizing your portfolio process every 18-24 months when the next executive shuffle happens. Departments come and go like spring fashion trends, but the fundamental ways your organization creates value? Those stick around.

Think of it like the difference between a city's political boundaries and its actual neighborhoods. Political boundaries tell you who's in charge of what right now. Neighborhoods tell you where people actually live, work, and create value. When you're trying to manage investments and optimize how business gets done, you need the neighborhood perspective - not the political map.
### Domains as the stable foundation

Business domains provide the **bounded contexts** for your business enterprise architecture. They're logical containers that represent stable ways your organization creates value though the execution of [[Value Streams]]:

- **Back office domains** - The usual cast of Finance, Human Resources, Legal, Information Technology, Facilities. These enable your organization to operate but don't directly touch customers. They're essential, often under appreciated, and surprisingly stable across organizational restructures.
- **Front office domains** - Customer-facing functions like Sales, Marketing, Service Delivery, Customer Support. These domains directly impact customer experience and revenue generation. They change in how they operate, but the fundamental "what" remains consistent.
- **Extended domains** - Digital channels, partner integrations, and other extensions that take your core business capabilities and extend them to interact with stakeholders in new ways. These emerged from the CRM and digital transformation movements and continue to evolve rapidly.
- **Planning and strategy domains** - Strategic planning, business intelligence, risk management, innovation management. These domains help your organization decide where to invest and how to evolve.

Most medium to large organizations end up with 20-30 domains when they create their one-page [[Enterprise Domain Model]]. 

### Domains contain value streams

Here's where it gets interesting for portfolio management: domains are collections of **value streams**. Each value stream represents an end-to-end flow of value creation for a specific stakeholder. For example:

- A Finance domain might contain value streams like "Process Payment"
- A Sales domain might contain Acquire New Customer, Manage Sales Opportunity
- An HR domain might contain "Hire New Employee," "Develop Employee Skills," "Manage Benefits"

Value streams are where the work actually happens. They're triggered by business events, flow through stages of value creation, and deliver outcomes to stakeholders. When you approve an idea, you're ultimately improving or creating value streams. When you invest in improving or creating new business capabilities, you're enabling value streams to execute more effectively.
### Why this matters for idea portfolios

When you organize idea portfolios around business domains rather than departments, several things happen:

- **Stability** - Your portfolio management process doesn't need to be reorganized every time there's an org chart shuffle. The SVP of Sales retires and three departments get merged? Your Sales domain and its value streams remain unchanged.
- **Clear accountability** - Each domain has clear ownership of its value streams. When an idea impacts multiple domains due to your organization's [[Value Stream Network]], you know exactly which domain teams need to collaborate on the assessment.
- **Value-focused** - Because domains organize around value streams, portfolio decisions naturally focus on business outcomes rather than organizational politics. "Will this improve how we serve customers?" beats "Does this make the VP look good?"
- **Natural boundaries** - Domains provide natural boundaries for portfolio scope. A domain portfolio team can focus on ideas that impact their value streams without drowning in every idea across the entire enterprise.
### Domain portfolios vs. enterprise portfolios

In practice, most organizations end up with a hybrid model:

- **Domain-level portfolios** - Each domain (or sometimes groups of related domains) manages its own portfolio of ideas and investments. The Finance domain manages ideas that primarily impact Finance value streams. The HR domain manages workforce-related initiatives.
- **Enterprise portfolio aggregations** - Domain portfolios roll up into higher-level views, often organized by the same back office/front office/extended framework. This gives executives visibility across multiple domains without requiring every idea to go through a single, monolithic approval process.
- **Cross-domain coordination** - Some ideas span multiple domains (most transformative ones do). These need coordination mechanisms between domain portfolios, but the assessment work can be distributed to the domains that have the expertise.

Think of domain portfolios as the intelligence agencies in a spy thriller. Each agency has its own mission, resources, and area of expertise. But when something big is happening, they coordinate through established channels. The alternative - one massive central agency trying to manage everything - leads to bureaucratic paralysis. We've all seen that movie, and it doesn't end well.
### The domain-first approach for this article

For the rest of this article, when we talk about "idea portfolios," assume we're talking about **domain portfolios** unless explicitly stated otherwise. The Kanban board we'll describe works at the domain level. The approval processes align to domain governance. The roadmaps connect to improving one or more domain value streams.

If your organization hasn't established its business domains yet, start there. Create your one-page Enterprise Domain Model, identify which value streams belong to each domain, and establish domain portfolio teams with clear accountability. Then you'll have the stable foundation needed to manage ideas effectively.

Or you can keep organizing around your org chart and rewrite this entire portfolio process again next year when Finance and IT get merged. Your choice.
## Managing organizational investments - Art or science?

Every organization has more ideas than it has capacity to implement them. It's like being at an all-you-can-eat buffet armed with nothing but a teaspoon - everything looks delicious, but you can only carry so much at once. The question isn't whether ideas are good; it's which ideas are worth implementing right now, and which ones should gracefully die on the vine.

Ideas for solving organizational problems come in all shapes and sizes, from the trivial ("Can we change the font in our presentations?") to the transformative ("Let's completely reimagine how we serve customers"). Organizations need ideas to continuously improve, transform, and innovate. But here's the uncomfortable truth: the bigger the organization, the more likely your brilliant idea will suffocate under the weight of governance processes that consume more resources than just implementing the damn thing and letting it fail fast.

Balancing what might look like absolute anarchy with the ability to measure and manage investments that demonstrably contribute to strategic goals requires something approaching supernatural powers. Throw into that mix the seemingly ever-present need to chase the latest [[Stealth EA/Blog Entries/2024 Buzzword Bingo]], and you've got yourself a proper challenge.

## Approaches for managing Domain idea portfolios

The question of how many idea portfolios you're managing really comes down to the size of your organization and how much complexity you can stomach. Many organizations manage dedicated innovation portfolios with teams specifically oriented to experimenting with the new and novel - essentially giving smart people permission to play with shiny objects in a controlled environment.
### Top down

Many organizations spend enormous amounts of time trying to centrally capture all ideas and manage them through the idea lifecycle. This usually emerges from departments with constraints - Project Management Offices, Legal, Privacy, Enterprise Architecture, Finance - who understandably want visibility into what's coming at them. The result? A central repository that becomes the place where good ideas go to celebrate birthdays.
### Bottom up

There's a tendency to make initiatives as big as possible - because apparently "go big or go home" applies to enterprise architecture too. That said, many organizations are attempting more Agile approaches. While bottom-up idea management might still result in large initiatives, it has a better chance of shaping ideas into portfolios that could be implemented independently. You know, actually getting stuff done.
### Middle out

The middle-out approach usually aligns to some form of governance at the [[Stealth Enterprise Architecture/Enterprise Architecture/Business Architecture/Domain Architecture/Business Domain]] level. While domains may have subordinate working groups generating ideas, the approval mechanism typically sits at the domain portfolio level. This makes sense when you remember that domains (unlike departments) represent stable ways your organization creates value.
## The idea lifecycle

No matter which approach you take, the idea process usually follows a fairly standard lifecycle. Invariably, organizations end up with a hybrid approach - which makes sense because the world isn't a hierarchy, it's a network. But regardless of how many portfolios you're juggling, or how collaboratively (or not) those portfolio teams are working together, the general idea [[Value Stream]] remains essentially the same.

The Stealth EA recommends a Kanban approach to visualizing and managing idea work in progress. Why? Because Kanban makes work visible, limits work in progress, and forces conversations about priorities. Plus, executives love staring at boards with coloured sticky notes.
### Idea Kanban board

| Draft                         | Submitted                                                                           | Analysis                                                                                 | Approval                                                                                                                                                                                | On Hold                                                   | Closed                                                       |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Ideas waiting to be submitted | Ideas Submitted and waiting for prioritization by the appropriate Domain governance | Ideas that have started the [[Architecture Decision Request (aka SBAR)\|SBAR]] analysis. | Ideas that are awaiting approval (or not) before being added to the [[Domain Investment Portfolio Management\|Domain Roadmap]] and/or the affected [[Enterprise Agile Product Roadmap]] | Bureaucracy has helped many an idea celebrate a birthday. | Successful, or not, every idea must be closed at some point. |

1. **Draft** - This is where idea generators document their brilliant thoughts. Keep the form as short as possible and resist the temptation to make people fill out a 47-field requirements document just to suggest something. Starting with the end in mind, this should be as simple as:
	- **Idea situation** - What caused the submitter to think this was a good idea? (Translation: What's broken or what opportunity did you spot?)
	- **Background** - What context does the assessment team need to start their work?
	- **Impact** - If implemented, how much of an impact will this have on the organization? (Be honest. "Massive transformation" and "changes the font" are not equally impactful.)

2. **Submitted** - Now that the idea has been submitted, it needs some form of endorsement from one or more governance areas to proceed. At this stage it could be closed, put on hold, or - most commonly - sit in this state for months while everyone pretends they'll get to it eventually.
3. **Analysis** - Depending on the size and scope of the idea, once approved it needs some form of analysis. The Stealth EA prefers the [[Architecture Decision Request (aka SBAR)|RSBA (Recommendation, Situation, Background, Assessment)]] approach. Usually there's one domain that takes the lead because they hold the key to the value case. They then engage with other domains or sub-domains to assess the idea and make the appropriate recommendation.
	1. **Pro tip**: Always include the "do nothing" option in your assessment. Sometimes that's actually the right answer, and it helps you articulate why the other options are better than maintaining the status quo.
4. **Approval** - Once the analysis is complete, it needs approval from one or more domain portfolio management teams. This can be time-consuming. The Stealth EA has seen many an idea's analysis celebrate multiple birthdays getting through the governance process. If you find yourself singing "Happy Birthday dear SBAR" for the second time, something's wrong with your process.
5. **On hold** - For various reasons, idea assessments may be put on hold. This should have a time limit attached, after which you go back to the submitter to see if the idea still needs to stay open. Otherwise, your "on hold" queue becomes the place where ideas go to die slowly and painfully.
6. **Closed** - At the end, the idea is closed and can be approved, declined, rejected, or possibly refactored into smaller or multiple other ideas.

Tip This is critically important: Approval of an idea is **not** approval to implement! All domain or sub-domain portfolios have lots on their plates already. The idea needs to be sequenced within a [[Enterprise Agile Domain Roadmaps|domain roadmap]], and sometimes multiple domain roadmaps. Confusing "yes, that's a good idea" with "yes, start building that tomorrow" is how you end up with angry project managers and confused delivery teams.

### Idea attributes and supporting idea approval

The Stealth EA has seen all kinds of ways that ideas have had to run the gauntlet of data required to submit and track them. The usual suspects include:

- Business value or return on investment
- Feasibility or effort required
- Strategic alignment
- Employee impact
- Customer impact

These attributes are usually rather difficult to implement accurately, and while there are lots of pretty portfolio pictures and dashboards that could be generated, they often create more theatre than insight. Invariably, having some form of voting process on the RSBA is the simplest approach to implement. This can be as straightforward as documentation within meeting minutes for each voting member, or something more electronic if you're feeling fancy.

This does get more complicated if an idea needs multiple portfolio approvals. Welcome to the joys of enterprise architecture - where good ideas go to get consensus.

## Role of the Stealth EA in the idea portfolio process

The Stealth EA can play many different roles in this process. At minimum, having an idea's [[Solution Concept Model]] is usually valuable in understanding the scope of what's being evaluated. This may end up being many solution architectures depending on the number of alternatives being assessed.

The Stealth EA usually also operates within some form of architecture [[Community of Practice]], which means you're not working alone. Use your fellow architects for peer review, especially when you suspect decision-based evidence making is creeping into your analysis. Having someone who disagrees with your recommendation review your RSBA is painful but valuable - they'll spot the biases you're blind to.

Remember: Your job isn't to advocate for ideas or kill them. Your job is to provide clear, unbiased analysis that helps decision-makers be more successful. Do that consistently well, and people will actually want to see more of your work.
