I have had a fungal infection on my toenail for a number of years.  I had previously been treated with Terbinafine HCL  250mg, but no affect.  I have taken another doe recently once a day for 90 days.  How long will it take to see it that had an affect?  Also - what are some other options.  I have also tried a topical ointment that I think was efinaconazole.

## Answers

### How long until you see an effect from the 90-day terbinafine course?

The medication and the cosmetic result run on two very different clocks.

The drug itself works fast. Terbinafine reaches the nail within about a week of starting and stays in the nail at active levels for ~30 weeks after your last pill, so it keeps killing fungus well after you stop taking it. The infection in the nail bed is typically cleared during or shortly after the 12-week course.

The *appearance*, though, lags far behind. Terbinafine doesn't fix the nail that's already damaged — that discolored nail has to grow out and be replaced by clear nail growing in behind it. Toenails grow slowly (~1–1.5 mm/month), so a full toenail takes roughly **12–18 months** to replace itself. Expect to see new clear nail at the base within a few months, but full clearing of the visible nail can take up to a year or more after finishing the pills. The right way to judge whether it worked is to look for healthy clear nail at the cuticle (base) and measure it growing outward over time — not to expect the whole nail to look normal soon.

Cure rates for a 12-week course are roughly 70–85% (complete cure ~67–85% depending on duration), so it does fail in a meaningful minority.

### Important caveat — two failed courses is a flag

You've now had terbinafine *twice* with no effect. That pattern is worth raising with a doctor, because it can mean one of three things:

1. **It's not actually a dermatophyte fungus.** Discolored/thickened nails can be caused by non-dermatophyte molds, yeast, psoriasis, or old trauma — none of which respond to terbinafine. A **nail clipping sent for fungal culture or PCR** before committing to another course settles this and is the single most useful next step.
2. **Terbinafine-resistant fungus.** Resistant dermatophyte strains (e.g. *T. indotineae*) are increasingly common and won't clear with terbinafine at any dose. PCR testing can identify these.
3. **A dermatophytoma or very thick nail** — a dense fungal pocket or thick nail the drug can't penetrate, which often needs the nail (or part of it) physically removed first.

### Other options

**Itraconazole (oral)** — the usual next-line pill after terbinafine fails. Often given as pulse therapy (200 mg twice daily, one week per month, ~3 pulses for toenails). A sequential itraconazole-then-terbinafine approach has shown higher cure rates (~52% vs ~32%) than terbinafine pulses alone. Requires liver monitoring and has more drug interactions than terbinafine.

**Efinaconazole 10% (topical)** — the one you've tried. Worth knowing it has to be applied daily for 48 weeks (a full year) to work, and it's most effective on mild-to-moderate cases; many people stop too early. Resistant strains generally still respond to it. If you only used it briefly, that's likely why it didn't help.

**Other topicals** — ciclopirox 8% lacquer and tavaborole 5% are alternatives, but topicals in general have lower cure rates than oral pills for thick/severe nails.

**Removing the nail (avulsion)** — partial or full removal of the affected nail before/with an oral antifungal can push cure rates toward ~100% for single-nail or dermatophytoma cases, by getting rid of the fungal reservoir the drug can't reach.

**Laser therapy** — Nd:YAG and other lasers are FDA-cleared only for *temporary* increase in clear nail. Evidence for actually curing the fungus is weak. Generally not recommended as a primary treatment and usually not covered by insurance.

**Bottom line to discuss with a podiatrist/dermatologist:** get a nail culture or PCR first to confirm what you're actually treating and whether it's resistant, then choose between itraconazole, a properly completed full year of topical efinaconazole, or nail removal plus an oral agent.

*Not medical advice — confirm with your doctor before changing treatment.*
