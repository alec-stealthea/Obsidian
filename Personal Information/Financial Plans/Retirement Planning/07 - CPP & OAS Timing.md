## OAS timing
- Full OAS at 65: **$8,916.60/yr** (you have 43 residency years → full benefit). Can defer to 70 for +36%.
- **Clawback begins at $95,323 net income (2026)**, 15¢ per $1 above, fully eliminated ~$152,062 (age 65–74). [Source: CRA recovery tax].
- **This is NOT comfortably below the floor — it's right at it.** Your *individual* income if CPP + OAS both start at 65:
  - **Retire 2030:** pension $67,700 + CPP $18,092 + OAS $8,917 ≈ **$94,700** — just under the $95,323 line (≈ $0 clawback, but no headroom).
  - **Retire 2032:** pension $77,600 + CPP $18,092 + OAS $8,917 ≈ **$104,600** — over by ~$9,300 → **~$1,400/yr OAS clawed back.**
  - **After age 71**, RRIF minimum withdrawals add taxable income on top of either, pushing both over the threshold.
- **Implication:** deferring CPP keeps income at pension + OAS ≈ $76,600 through the late 60s — under the threshold, full OAS retained, and room to draw down the RRSP at low rates before RRIF minimums hit. The earlier file note ("likely below the clawback floor") was too optimistic; clawback is a live planning item, especially under the 2032 path and after 71.

## Scenario: Take CPP now (age 61) and redirect it to your RRSP

*The idea: start CPP today while still working, and contribute the after-receipt amount to your RRSP so the deduction offsets the CPP income — making it tax-neutral now and pushing the tax bill into lower-rate retirement years.*

**The mechanics, with your numbers:**
- CPP now at 61: **$13,315.56/yr** (your MSCA figure). Redirect the full amount to the RRSP each year and the deduction cancels the CPP income on your return.
- Collected early before retirement: **~$53,300 to a 2030 retirement** (4 yrs) or **~$79,900 to 2032** (6 yrs).
- Tax-deferral value *if the RRSP room is otherwise spare*: roughly **~$1,300/yr** — deferring ~43% top-bracket tax now and paying ~33% on withdrawal later (≈ a 10-point bracket spread on $13,315).

**Why this is weaker than it first looks — three structural catches:**
1. **CPP is not earned income, so taking it creates no new RRSP room.** The redirect just consumes part of your existing **$90,000 unused room** (file 08) — the same room you were already going to fill from your ~$200k salary at the top bracket. Six years of CPP would eat ~$80k of it. Net *new* tax shelter created ≈ **zero** unless you genuinely can't fill that room from salary/savings anyway. If you can fill it from salary, the salary deduction is worth more (higher marginal rate) than the CPP deduction.
2. **The early start is a permanent, indexed haircut.** Taking at 61 locks in **~$4,776/yr less than the age-65 amount, and ~$12,375/yr less than the age-70 amount — for life, fully CPI-indexed.** That is the exact opposite of the deferral logic above (indexed longevity insurance, OAS-clawback headroom). The early-take break-even is ~age 74; with your planning age of 90 (file 01), the longevity math favours waiting.
3. **Mandatory contributions continue.** Taking CPP while working before 65 means you must keep paying CPP premiums (these earn a small Post-Retirement Benefit) — a minor offset, not a saver.

**Where this scenario *can* make sense:**
- As an **early-death hedge / estate play.** CPP dies with you; an RRSP is an inheritable asset. Given your dad passed at 83 (file 01), monetizing CPP early into an estate-accessible account has some appeal if you weight early-death risk heavily.
- If you find yourself with **RRSP room you genuinely cannot fill** from salary or savings before retirement — then redirected CPP at least shelters money that would otherwise sit taxable.

**Net read on this scenario:**
> The tax deferral is real but small (~$1,300/yr) and gated on having spare RRSP room — which you likely don't, since salary already targets the $90k. Against that you give up a large, permanent, indexed CPP reduction. **This is a liquidity/estate decision, not a tax-optimization win**, and it runs directly counter to the deferral recommendation below. Only favour it if you specifically want early cash, are hedging early death, or have unfillable RRSP room.

## Recommendation (preliminary)
> **Lean toward deferring CPP toward 70** given the indexed pension floor, the clawback math, and longevity-as-insurance logic — *provided* savings/spending (files 03/04) let you bridge the gap without strain. The **take-CPP-now-and-redirect-to-RRSP** scenario above does *not* change this: its tax-deferral upside is small and gated on spare RRSP room, while the permanent indexed reduction is large — treat it as a liquidity/estate hedge, not a default. **Take OAS at 65** under the 2030 path (you keep essentially all of it); under 2032, model whether a short OAS deferral or RRSP-drawdown sequencing avoids the small clawback.
