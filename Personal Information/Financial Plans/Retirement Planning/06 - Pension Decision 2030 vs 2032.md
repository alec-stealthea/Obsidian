## The question
Work to your unreduced pension age (**2030**) and retire, or work two more years to **2032** for a materially higher pension? This file quantifies the financial gap and weighs it against two years of life.

## What drives the difference (capture sources in file 02)
Working 2030 → 2032 typically raises the pension through:
1. **More pensionable service** — two additional accrual years.
2. **Higher highest-average salary** — if final years are your top-earning years.
3. **No/less early-reduction** — confirm 2030 is truly unreduced; if not, the reduction removal alone can be large.
4. **Two more years of full-salary saving** — extra RRSP/TFSA/cash, not just pension.
5. **Shorter drawdown horizon** — savings fund 2 fewer years.

## Break-even analysis

- **The trade:** retire 2030 and you bank two early years of pension (2030 + 2031 ≈ $136,000) that the 2032 plan never collects. From 2032 on, the higher 2032 pension out-earns the 2030 pension by ~$7,900/yr (growing with indexing) and slowly erases that early lead.
- **Break-even age ≈ 82–83** (the higher 2032 pension overtakes 2030's cumulative total around **2047**). Live past ~83 and working to 2032 wins on pension dollars alone; die before it and retiring in 2030 was the better financial call.
- **Longevity flag:** your dad passed at 83 (file 01) — squarely on the break-even age. That's the single most important personal input here. If you weight your own longevity below ~85, the pure pension math tilts toward **2030**.
- **What pushes it back toward 2032:** the two extra years of full salary let you save more and shorten the drawdown by two years — neither is in the pension-only break-even above. Quantifying that needs your savings rate and spending floor (files 03/04).
- **Sensitivity:** higher inflation widens the nominal gap but barely moves the real gap; a higher real return on savings strengthens 2030 (you can invest the early pension); a lower planning age strengthens 2030.

- Two years of go-go-phase health (you're in good shape now; you've started strength training) vs. ~$23k of real lifetime pension. Is each of those two years worth more than ~$11.5k to you?
