# 13 — Scenario: Transfer House to Daughter (2030)

*Scenario / analysis file. Explores transferring our home to our daughter at retirement, financing it as a loan back to us at the Bank of Canada rate, and keeping the estate equitable across all four children. Feeds into [[10 - Estate & Insurance]] and [[11 - Scenarios & Cash Flow Model]].*

> **Not formal advice.** I'm not a lawyer, accountant, or tax advisor. Intra-family property transfers have real tax and legal traps (below). Treat this as decision-support and confirm the specifics with an Alberta real-estate lawyer + an accountant before acting.

> [!update] Revised variant added (June 2026)
> Sections 1–9 below were the first pass (loan at the **BoC rate**, daughter's use of the house open). The newer, preferred variant — **Haley moves in, sells her house, mortgage set at *market* rate, and we gift down her balance** — is worked out in the new section **["Variant B: Haley moves in + market-rate mortgage + gift"](#variant-b-haley-moves-in--market-rate-mortgage--gift)** at the bottom. That section answers "how much can we gift?"

## The idea in one line

At retirement (2030) we hand our **$1,700,000** home to our daughter, downsize into a **~$900,000** home for ourselves, and structure the difference as a **loan to her at the Bank of Canada rate** so we collect monthly income — while keeping our three sons whole in the estate plan.

## The key tension to understand first

You can't get **both** a lump of cash *and* a monthly income stream out of the same dollars. The $800,000 you free up by downsizing can either:

- **(a)** stay as cash/investments in your hands, **or**
- **(b)** be lent to your daughter (a "vendor-take-back" mortgage) so she pays you monthly.

Whatever you lend her, you no longer have to spend. And critically: **you still need $900,000 in cash to buy your own downsized home.** Where that $900k comes from drives the whole structure. The realistic versions:

### Option A — Daughter gets a bank mortgage; you take cash (no monthly income)
- She buys at FMV $1.7M: ~$200k of her own equity down + ~$1.5M bank mortgage.
- You receive ~$1.7M cash → buy your $900k home → ~$800k left to invest for retirement.
- *You get a lump sum, not monthly income. Simplest and cleanest for estate fairness.*

### Option B — You lend her the swing amount (monthly income, the structure you asked about)
- She gives you ~$900k upfront (her ~$200k equity + a ~$700k bank mortgage) → you buy your $900k home.
- You hold a **$800,000 loan (VTB mortgage)** on the old house at the BoC rate; she pays you monthly.
- *You get monthly income; the $800k stays locked in her house instead of invested elsewhere.*

### Option C — You lend her the whole thing (largest income, needs your own savings)
- She puts ~$200k down; you hold a **~$1.5M** VTB at the BoC rate.
- But you only received $200k, so you must fund the other **~$700k of your $900k home from your own RRSP/TFSA/non-registered savings** (or a small mortgage on the new place).
- *Only works if you have ~$700k of liquid retirement assets you're willing to redeploy.*

**Most likely fit: Option B.** The natural amount to lend is the **$800,000 swing**, because that's the capital downsizing actually frees up.

## Monthly income at the Bank of Canada rate (2.25%)

What 2.25% produces depends on whether she only pays interest, or pays interest **and** principal (amortizing).

**On an $800,000 loan (Option B):**

| Structure | Monthly to you | Annual | Taxable portion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Interest-only | **$1,500** | $18,000 | All $18,000 is taxable interest |
| 25-yr amortizing | **$3,489** | $41,869 | Only the interest slice (~$18k yr 1, falling) |
| 20-yr amortizing | $4,142 | $49,710 | Interest slice only |

**On a $1,500,000 loan (Option C), for comparison:**

| Structure | Monthly to you | Annual |
|---|---|---|
| Interest-only | $2,812 | $33,750 |
| 25-yr amortizing | $6,542 | $78,504 |

Notes:
- With an **amortizing** loan the monthly cheque is bigger, but most of the early payment is just your own principal coming back — only the interest is income (and only the interest is taxable).
- **Interest you receive is taxable** at your marginal rate each year; return of principal is not. This adds to retirement taxable income — cross-check against OAS clawback and drawdown order in [[08 - Tax Strategy & Drawdown Order]].

## The catch with "the Bank of Canada rate"

At **2.25%**, you'd be lending far below what a bank charges for a mortgage (~4–5%+) and likely below what the same $800k could earn invested. That gap is real money you're giving up — i.e. a **gift to your daughter in the form of cheap interest**, even if she repays every dollar of principal:

| If market mortgage rate is… | Annual "discount" gift to her (yr 1, on $800k) |
|---|---|
| 4.0% | ~$14,000 / yr |
| 5.0% | ~$22,000 / yr |
| 5.5% | ~$26,000 / yr |

Over a 25-year amortizing $800k loan, she pays ~**$247k** in interest at 2.25% vs ~**$603k** at 5% — roughly **$356,000** of lifetime interest she *doesn't* pay. **This is the number to keep in mind for fairness to your sons** (see below). It's a benefit to her whether or not you ever forgive the principal.

> Note on family-loan rates: CRA's **prescribed-rate** rules (income attribution) mainly bite on loans to a *spouse* or *minor* — not to an adult child — so attribution isn't the worry here. The issue is purely the gift-equivalent value of the below-market rate, which matters for estate equalization, not for your own tax.

## Tax & legal traps to confirm (Alberta / Canada)

1. **Principal residence exemption (PRE) is your friend.** If the $1.7M home has been your principal residence for all the years you've owned it, the capital gain on the transfer is **exempt — no tax to you on the transfer**, even though it's deemed to happen at FMV. Confirm the property qualifies for all years.
2. **Deemed disposition at FMV — you can't dodge it with a low price.** A gift or non-arm's-length sale is treated as happening at **FMV ($1.7M)** regardless of what's actually paid. With PRE this is fine for you.
3. **Avoid the "double-tax trap": transfer at full FMV, don't lowball.** If you "sell" it to her cheaply (say $900k), you're still deemed to dispose at $1.7M (PRE covers it), **but her cost base becomes only $900k** — so she inherits a built-in $800k future capital gain. Transfer/sell at **full $1.7M FMV** (or gift outright) so her cost base is $1.7M. A lowball price is the worst of both worlds.
4. **Her future PRE.** If she *moves into* the house, it becomes her principal residence and future growth is sheltered for her. If she keeps it as a **rental**, future gains are taxable to her.
5. **Her current house.** When she sells it, her own PRE should shelter the gain. (If she rents it out instead, that changes.)
6. **Alberta has no land transfer tax** — only modest land-title registration fees. A real advantage vs Ontario/BC. (No GST on used residential housing; Canada has no gift tax.)
7. **Document the loan properly.** Register a real mortgage/promissory note with a rate, term, and payment schedule. This protects you (creditor/divorce claims against her), keeps the interest income clean, and makes the estate math defensible.

## Making it equitable with the three sons

The fairness problem: your daughter receives a $1.7M asset. Even with a real loan, the **below-market rate (~$356k of foregone interest)** — and especially any **forgiveness of the remaining balance on death** — is a transfer of value to one child. Options, roughly least-to-most generous to her:

1. **Treat the loan as genuine debt.** She actually repays principal + interest. The estate then holds a **mortgage receivable** that's divided among all four. Only the rate discount is a "gift" — smallest equalization needed.
2. **Equalize in the will (hotchpot / advancement clause).** State explicitly that the value she received (the gift portion, or any forgiven balance) is an **advance on her share**, and divide the residual estate to bring all four children to equal.
3. **Life insurance to offset.** Hold a policy naming the three sons, sized to the benefit given to the daughter. Clean, liquid, and avoids forcing asset sales — pairs well with [[10 - Estate & Insurance]].

**The equalization formula** (for the will), where *G* = the gift portion already given to your daughter and *Estate* = everything left to divide:

> Equal share each = (Estate + G) ÷ 4
> Daughter receives from estate: (Estate + G) ÷ 4 − G
> Each son receives: (Estate + G) ÷ 4

*Worked example* — if the residual estate is $2.0M and the gift portion G is the $800k VTB forgiven on death:
- Pool = $2.0M + $0.8M = $2.8M → equal share **$700k each**.
- The three sons each get **$700k** (= all $2.1M of the residual).
- The daughter's estate share is $700k − $800k = **−$100k**, i.e. she's already received $100k *more* than an equal share, so she gets nothing further and is, in fact, $100k ahead. To make it exactly equal you'd forgive only ~$700k, or top up the sons another ~$33k each from insurance.

The point: **decide now what counts as "her share"** — full FMV, the rate discount only, or the forgiven balance — and write the will to true everyone up to that definition.

# Variant B: Haley moves in + market-rate mortgage + gift

*Preferred variant. Haley moves into the $1.7M home as her principal residence and sells her own house; we set her mortgage at the **current market rate** (so there's no below-market "rate gift" for CRA to question); and we use her mortgage payments to cover a mortgage on our downsized home. Question answered: **how much can we gift her to shrink her mortgage?***

## What changes from the first pass

- **Haley lives in the house** → it becomes *her* principal residence, so her future growth is sheltered, and selling her current house should be **PRE-exempt** for her. Good outcomes both ways.
- **Mortgage at market rate (~4.0%)** instead of 2.25% → removes the "cheap-rate gift" issue from Section 4. (The FMV / cost-base rule in Section 3 still applies — **transfer at full $1.7M FMV**.)
- **Self-funding loop:** Haley pays us on her mortgage (a vendor-take-back held by us); we use that to pay the mortgage on our new $900k home.

## The answer: gift ≈ your leftover equity

The most you can gift while staying whole is simply:

> **Max gift = (home equity at transfer) − (cost of your new home)**

Because any gift beyond that means Haley's payments no longer cover your mortgage, and you'd be topping it up out of savings or other retirement income.

**The catch — you still owe on the house.** Per [[05 - Debts & Liabilities]] you owe ~$518.5k today at 4.04%; paying it down through ~2030 leaves an estimated **~$463k** balance that must be discharged when the house transfers. So:

| | Amount |
|---|---|
| Home FMV (2030) | $1,700,000 |
| − Existing mortgage to discharge (est. 2030) | −$463,000 |
| = **Equity at transfer** | **~$1,237,000** |
| − Your new home | −$900,000 |
| = **Maximum gift to Haley (self-funding)** | **≈ $337,000 (~$340k)** |

**If you clear the existing mortgage before 2030**, equity becomes the full $1.7M and the ceiling rises to **$1,700,000 − $900,000 = $800,000.**

> Rule of thumb: **Max gift ≈ $800,000 − (your remaining mortgage at transfer).** Every dollar you knock off your own mortgage before retiring is roughly a dollar more you can hand Haley.

## What the gift does to Haley's mortgage

Haley's loan = $1.7M − her $200k down payment − the gift. At a 4.0% market rate, 25-year amortization:

| Gift | Haley's mortgage | Haley's payment | Feasible while you stay whole? |
|---|---|---|---|
| $0 | $1,500,000 | $7,918 /mo | Yes (you keep her full payment stream) |
| $200,000 | $1,300,000 | $6,862 /mo | Yes |
| **~$340,000** | **$1,160,000** | **$6,123 /mo** | **Yes — ceiling at current ~$463k mortgage** |
| $500,000 | $1,000,000 | $5,278 /mo | Only if existing mortgage largely cleared |
| $800,000 | $700,000 | $3,695 /mo | Only if existing mortgage fully cleared first |

*(Market rate as of late June 2026: best 5-yr fixed ~3.94–4.16%, Big-6 average ~4.93% — see Sources. Re-quote at transfer.)*

## How the self-funding loop works

At the ~$340k-gift ceiling, the mechanics line up cleanly:

- You take a mortgage on your **$900k home** equal to what Haley still owes you on the **vendor-take-back**, so **her monthly payment ≈ your monthly payment** at the same rate/term. The money passes through you.
- Haley's total financing (~$1.16M) splits between a bank mortgage (cash she hands you at closing so you can discharge your $463k loan and fund your home) and the vendor-take-back you hold. Your lawyer structures the exact split; the *net* result is the table above.
- Result: Haley owns the $1.7M home with ~$540k of equity (her $200k + your $340k gift); you own your $900k home; your mortgage payment is covered by hers; your savings stay untouched.

> **Want a bigger gift than ~$340k?** Three levers: (1) **pay down your mortgage before 2030** (each dollar lifts the ceiling ~$1), (2) buy a **cheaper** replacement home (each $1 under $900k lifts the ceiling ~$1), or (3) **use savings** to fund part of the gift (but that's drawdown capital — check against [[11 - Scenarios & Cash Flow Model]]).

## Still must be equalized for the three sons

A gift of ~$340k (or up to $800k) to Haley is a real, large transfer of value — even though she pays market rate on what's left. It needs to be squared with your three sons via the will. Use the equalization formula and life-insurance option in Section "Making it equitable with the three sons" above: treat the **gift amount** (plus any VTB balance you forgive on death) as Haley's advance, and bring the others up to equal.
