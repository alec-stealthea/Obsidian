## The two potential inheritances

### A. Alec's mother
| Field | Value / assumption |
|---|---|
| Age now | 82, **excellent health** |
| Asset | Home, **~$800,000, mortgage-free** |
| Plan | **Sells the home and moves into a retirement home**, funded by her **OAS + CPP + her ¼ share** of the sale proceeds. The remaining proceeds are **gifted to her three children while she is alive.** |
| Trigger | Sells when she can no longer live independently — **Alec's assumption: ~age 90 (≈2034)** |
| Split | **Four ways at sale** — mother keeps a share; Alec + brother + sister each get a share |
| **Alec's likely share** | **~$200,000** (¼ of $800k), around **2034**, received as a **living gift** |

Notes and caveats:
- **The sale itself is tax-free, and so are the gifts.** A principal residence is exempt from capital-gains tax on sale (principal residence exemption), and **cash gifts carry no tax in Canada** (no gift tax). Her whole plan — sell, fund her care, gift the rest — has essentially no tax friction.
- She **keeps her own quarter** to live on. **Size that share conservatively:** retirement-home and later assisted-living/memory-care costs climb fast ($5–8k+/month), gifts are irreversible, and excellent health means a long runway to fund. Her share — and potentially more of the proceeds — gets consumed first if care needs rise. The $200k to each child is a ceiling, not a floor.
- **Excellent health at 82 cuts both ways.** It's wonderful, and it likely pushes the inflow *later* than 2034 (she could comfortably live past 90). Plan for "sometime in the 2030s," not a fixed year.
- This almost certainly arrives **after** Alec's retirement on either date, so it doesn't change the retire-2030-vs-2032 math — it just adds portfolio cushion afterward.

### B. Andrea's parents
| Field | Value / assumption |
|---|---|
| Mom | 85, reasonable health but **losing mobility** |
| Dad | 84, in a retirement home, **early dementia + wheelchair after a stroke** |
| **Both parents still alive** | First death triggers a **tax-free spousal rollover**; the tax event lands on the **second (last) death** |
| Estate | **$2–4M**, split **three ways**. Composition now known: **~$1.5M home + the remainder in a RRIF** |
| **Andrea's likely share** | **~$667k–$1.33M** (⅓ of $2–4M, **gross of RRIF tax**) |
| Trigger | Estate settles after **both** parents pass; timing uncertain |

Notes and caveats:
- **This is the larger and more uncertain of the two.** A $0.67M–$1.33M range is enormous spread; the plan should never assume the top end.
- **The RRIF is the tax landmine — and it shrinks the net estate before anyone inherits.** The home is sheltered (spousal rollover on the first death, then principal-residence exemption — tax-free throughout). The RRIF is not: on the **second** death the entire remaining balance is taxed as **income** in a single year, at close to Alberta's **~48% top marginal rate**. That tax is the *estate's* liability, paid before distribution, so it directly reduces Andrea's ⅓.
- **They are not drawing the RRIF down** — they spend less than they earn — so the RRIF keeps **growing**, and the eventual second-death tax bill grows with it. This argues *for* a voluntary **RRIF "meltdown"** while both are alive: draw extra income each year across **both** parents' lower brackets (with pension-income splitting), up to the **OAS clawback threshold (~$95k each, 2026)**, and redirect what they don't spend into **TFSAs / non-registered** savings. It's a tax-timing play, not a spending need — and the beneficiary is ultimately the kids' inheritance. *This is the in-laws' decision, and it only works while both are alive with two sets of lower brackets to use.*
- **Estate liquidity matters for the RRIF tax.** If the RRIF is paid to named beneficiaries while the tax is owed by the estate, and the estate is short on cash, CRA can pursue the beneficiaries personally for the tax. Make sure the estate keeps enough liquidity to cover the RRIF tax, or route the RRIF through the estate.
- **Dad's continuing care is currently fully covered by the Alberta government** — so it is *not* eroding the estate today. Two watch-items remain: (1) publicly-funded continuing care usually still carries a **resident accommodation ("room & board") charge** the resident pays, and (2) coverage rules and the level/type of care can change, or Mom may later need care that's less fully covered. So care risk is reduced, not eliminated.
- **The ~$1.5M home is illiquid** until sold, which affects settlement timing.
- Given both parents are mid-80s with meaningful health issues, this inflow is **plausibly within the next ~5–10 years** — i.e., it could land *before or around* Alec's retirement — but it is genuinely unpredictable.

## Routing decision — receive it, then gift; don't restructure the wills

**Decision:** Do **not** ask either set of parents to rewrite their wills to pass cash directly to the four grandchildren. Alec and Andrea will **receive their shares and then gift to the kids at their own discretion** if and when it makes sense.

Why this is the right call:
- **No tax benefit is lost.** Canada has no inheritance or gift tax, so a direct-to-grandchildren bequest saves nothing at the point of transfer versus receiving and gifting.
- **The one structural advantage is preserved anyway.** Routing directly to the kids would avoid a *second* deemed disposition in Alec/Andrea's own estate — but that benefit is captured simply by **gifting the money along reasonably promptly** rather than letting it sit and compound in their hands for years.
- **More flexibility, less complexity.** Receiving first lets them size and time gifts to circumstances at the time (the kids' ages, needs, the household's own position), and avoids coordinating will changes across two families.

**Condition:** the equivalence holds **only if gifts are made reasonably promptly.** Money that sits and grows for years in Alec/Andrea's hands re-enters their own estate for the second-generation tax drag and their own creditor/matrimonial exposure.

**Keep separate:** this routing decision is independent of the **RRIF drawdown** issue above. Simplifying the wills costs nothing in tax — but the in-laws' RRIF still keeps growing untaxed, and that (not how the money is routed) is where the real dollars are.

## How this affects the retirement plan

**1. It strengthens the case for retiring in 2030, not 2032.**
The main reason to work to 2032 is portfolio cushion and a bigger pension. If a meaningful inheritance is reasonably likely in the 2030s, the marginal two years of work buy *less* incremental security than they appear to. This doesn't decide it — but it tilts the non-financial scales (file 01: boredom risk vs. fulfilling work) toward "you can afford to choose freely."

**2. It's a tax-free inflow in Canada — to you, but the estate's own tax can be large.**
Canada has **no inheritance or estate tax paid by the recipient.** Inherited cash or property is **not taxable income** to Alec or Andrea. But the *estate* settles its tax before distribution, and that can be substantial: the in-laws' **RRIF is fully taxed as income on the second death (~48%)**, which meaningfully reduces Andrea's net share. A principal residence is exempt. So "tax-free to you" is true at the recipient line, but the **headline estate value is not what lands** — net out the RRIF tax. *Confirm specifics with CRA / the estate's accountant — see references.*

**3. But it creates future taxable income once invested.**
A $1M+ inflow invested in non-registered accounts throws off interest/dividends/gains that **count toward income and can trigger OAS clawback** (threshold ~$95k in 2026, file 07). When/if these land, revisit the drawdown order (file 08) — e.g., this may argue for spending down RRSP/RRIF earlier and letting the inheritance backfill, to manage lifetime tax and clawback.

**4. It can fund the things file 01 cares about.**
The grandchildren contribution, the house-to-daughter idea (file 13), travel in the go-go years, and any charitable legacy all become easier. An inheritance is the natural funding source for *gifting while living* rather than just passing assets on again.

**5. Don't forget the cost side of aging parents.**
These are inflows, but the same situations can bring **outflows and time**: travel to help, care coordination, or topping up a parent's care costs. Andrea may carry a caregiving load for her parents in the same window. Budget some flexibility for this, not just the windfall.

## How to model it (recommended)

- **Keep it out of Scenarios 1–4** in file 11. The base feasibility of 2030 vs 2032 must not depend on it.
- **Add an "Inheritance overlay" scenario (Scenario 6):** take Scenario 1 (retire 2030) and drop in a **conservative** lump sum — e.g., $400k mid-decade — to see how much earlier the portfolio stops being a constraint and how it changes the age-90 legacy number. Run a low case ($200k) and a high case ($1M+) as bookends.
- **Headline to watch:** with even the *low* inheritance case layered on, does the plan move from "feasible" to "feasible with large surplus / gifting capacity"? That surplus is the real decision-relevant output — it's optionality, not spending need.
